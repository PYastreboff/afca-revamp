Key complaint trends

Modest increase in complaints

AFCA received 4,648 complaints from small businesses in 2024‑25, a 4% increase on 2023‑24. The rise reflects continued pressure on small businesses, as they navigate higher operating costs and economic uncertainty, as well as an increased focus on account operation and service quality.

In January 2024, we published AFCA’s Approach to Lending to Small Business. It tells borrowers and lenders how we make decisions when small businesses complain that they should not have received a loan as it was not affordable. In 2024‑25, we actively sought feedback on how the Approach was working in practice. The limited feedback received suggests the Approach is helping parties to resolve disputes and better understand AFCA outcomes.

Rise in business transaction account complaints

Complaints about business transaction accounts cover issues like delays in lifting restrictions on accounts, operation of terms and conditions, or account closures. Business transaction accounts were the most complained about product in 2024‑25, rising 32% to 1,676 – the largest year‑on‑year increase across small business product types.

Service quality and scams key issues

Service quality complaints cover issues like delays in assessing a loan application, or relationship manager turnover that leads to non‑continuous customer assistance. Service quality complaints increased to 337 in 2024‑25, closer to the five‑year average, after a dip in 2023‑24. Failure to respond to requests for assistance remained a key concern with 310 complaints. Scam complaints rose slightly to 286.

Resolution and timeframes

More complaints resolved, faster

AFCA closed 4,817 small business complaints this year, a 10% increase on 2023‑24. Average resolution time was 97 days, down from 101. More than 40% were resolved at Referral.

Industry trends and challenges

For the past few years, small businesses have found the economic environment very challenging. Growth in demand for products and services has slowed in some sectors while input costs remain elevated, putting pressure on profitability. This is hardest for businesses reliant on discretionary consumer spending such as entertainment and hospitality. Access to credit is challenging and many small businesses are seeking finance from non‑bank lenders. Some small businesses, that are not performing well, have exhausted their cash buffers and entered insolvency. This group is rising, but insolvencies are still below pre‑pandemic trends. There has been an increase in small businesses appointing debt restructuring practitioners.

Small Business Strategy

AFCA has launched a new Small Business Strategy which outlines our areas of focus for the coming two years. Our objectives are to ensure that small businesses are aware we can assist with their disputes. We want to ensure our rules and operational guidelines support small business access to AFCA and ensure our service is efficient and timely.

As part of this we will be continuing our existing stakeholder engagement, but looking to engage with Government, industry regulators and advocates more broadly. We will be using our experience in small business claims to highlight areas where gaps exist in protection for small business.

The case studies illustrate our approach to some of the issues we have seen in business lending over the past financial year.

Case study

AFCA considers the substance of the facility in determining a copy of a bank guarantee is acceptable

Background

The complainant ran a manufacturing business from a leased building. The landlord required a bank guarantee as a form of security for the tenancy.

The complainant signed a bank guarantee facility, agreeing to pay an annual fee and provide security to the bank (among other things). In return, the bank gave the complainant an original bank guarantee to pass on to the landlord. It named the landlord as beneficiary.

When the complainant signed the lease, they kept the original bank guarantee and gave the landlord a copy. The landlord did not realise it was only a copy.

Complaint

A dispute arose between the complainant and the landlord. The landlord, following the process set out in the bank guarantee, provided the bank with a written demand for payment and a copy of the bank guarantee. Both the bank and the landlord thought they were dealing with the original bank guarantee document.

The bank paid the landlord the amount of the demand, then debited the complainant’s transaction account for same.

The complainant objected because the landlord had presented only a copy of the bank guarantee with the demand. The bank initially said the demand came with the original bank guarantee, but soon realised it was a copy. The bank also argued that the bank guarantee facility that the complainant had signed allowed the bank to act, based on the demand made, even if it was accompanied by only a copy of the bank guarantee document.

Outcome

An AFCA panel reviewed the terms of the bank guarantee facility which governed the relationship between the bank and the complainant, including:

The panel concluded that the bank could pay a demand made with only a copy of the bank guarantee. The facility even permitted the bank to pay the beneficiary, in some cases, without a demand being made.

AFCA has made similar determinations in other cases where a demand came with only a bank guarantee copy. When considering a fair outcome, AFCA considers the substance of the relationship between the parties. In this case, the bank guarantee facility was closely reviewed, ensuring the bank had acted in accordance with its contractual relationship with the complainant.

  • when the complainant agreed the bank may pay the beneficiary
  • when the complainant agreed to indemnify the bank for such payment.

Case study

Obligation to assess the capacity to repay when the AFIA Online Small Business Lenders code applies

Background

A company operated in the real estate industry. The husband was the sole director and the wife the sole shareholder. In July 2022, the financial firm (lender) provided the company with a business loan of $154,000 for working capital and fit out of new premises. Both husband and wife (complainant) provided personal guarantees for the loan.

From October 2022, the company failed to meet its repayment obligations to the lender. Default notices were issued in December 2022. A liquidator was appointed to the company in October 2023.

Complaint

The wife complained to AFCA that the company loan should not have been provided, and sought to be released from her guarantee.

As the loan was provided to a company, the National Credit Code did not apply. The lender was a subscriber to the Australian Finance Industry Association’s Online Small Business Lenders Code of Practice (AFIA Code). Under the AFIA Code, a lender can only lend to a small business if its assessment shows there is capacity to repay.

AFCA reviewed all information the lender had used to assess the loan application, which included the company’s profit or loss statement and balance sheet for the financial year ended 30 June 2022, Business Activity Statements, transaction statements and an ATO repayment plan. AFCA found that:

The lender should have realised the company was struggling financially and therefore, should have enquired further of its director. Instead, the lender processed its assessment to an approval, despite the clear overstatement of the company’s surplus monthly income. AFCA noted that the business’s deteriorating position led it to dishonour within three months of the loan being provided.

  • the company had trouble remitting collected GST and other statutory debt
  • there was a history of large incoming payments from other entities to cover expenses (rather than regular income from the company’s operations)
  • recent bank statements showed an inability to meet obligations on time
  • financial statements showed trading losses in March and April 2022.

Outcome

AFCA found the lender could not reasonably have concluded the loan would be repaid in due course, failing to meet their obligation under the AFIA Code. On that basis, AFCA determined the fairest outcome in the circumstances was for the wife to be released from her guarantee of the loan.

Case study

Financial difficulty assistance cannot be provided when the company borrower is deregistered

Background

In February 2022, a financial firm lent money to a company to acquire a vehicle. The company’s sole director (the complainant) signed a goods mortgage agreement with the financial firm. He also signed a guarantee for the loan.

In June 2022, the company was deregistered and vehicle ownership passed to ASIC (by operation of the Corporations Act). The complainant continued to use the vehicle, even though he had no rights to it, making occasional repayments until he was no longer able. He then sought financial difficulty assistance from the financial firm.

The financial firm was not a bank nor a signatory to an AFIA Code.

Complaint

The complainant said the financial firm had not provided him with financial difficulty assistance. He wanted to keep the vehicle and have more time to pay for it.

Outcome

The financial firm had no industry code obligations to either the company or the complainant as guarantor, but AFCA generally expects a financial firm to act fairly and reasonably, and genuinely consider a borrower’s financial difficulty.

However, in this case there was no longer a borrower. It had ceased to exist because of its deregistration. In cases like this, AFCA expects the financial firm to provide a short period of time for the guarantor (the complainant) to pay off the debt or arrange refinancing.

The determination was in favour of the financial firm because it had initially provided a three‑month moratorium on repayments followed by a four‑month moratorium. AFCA concluded the financial firm behaved fairly and reasonably by offering payment extensions. The financial firm owed limited obligations to a guarantor, and the guarantor accepted that his financial difficulties were long term.

AFCA was unable to deal with the complainant’s request to keep the vehicle. It became ASIC’s property the day the complainant’s company was deregistered.

¹ This excludes complaints that were inactive for an extended period. For example, complaints that were paused because the financial firm was insolvent or due to court proceedings, and complaints that were previously closed and then re‑opened.

Member FAQs

Definitions

AFCA’s Rules have been amended to introduce a definition of ‘Bank’ as “includes, but is not limited to, a body corporate that is an authorised deposit-taking institution (ADI) for the purposes of the Banking Act 1959.” AFCA’s definition has been adopted from the definition of ‘bank’ in the Corporations Act, in accordance with the Authorisation Condition.

AFCA acknowledges this is a broad definition and may include some non-ADI entities. AFCA encourages all financial firms to review the definition and seek clarification if they are unsure whether they are captured.

If you're a non-ADI member offering deposit banking like services accounts, it's possible you may receive complaints under the new jurisdiction. Where you offer these services through an arrangement with an underlying ADI (where the ADI is responsible for holding the deposit), we encourage you to start identifying and engaging with your ADI now. This will help ensure you're well-prepared to respond to any complaints when the changes take effect.

A receiving bank is a financial institution that receives funds transferred as part of a scam. These funds are generally deposited into accounts held or controlled (such as when the account is rented out or sold) by scammers to collect or act as a conduit for stolen money.

The expanded jurisdiction will allow AFCA to also examine the conduct of banks that received scam funds belonging to the complainant, whether the complainant is their customer or not.

An unauthorised account is an account opened, or a credit facility made available, by a bank in the complainant’s name, without the complainant’s consent or authority. Unauthorised accounts may be used as mule accounts.

Under the expanded jurisdiction, AFCA will be able to investigate complaints involving unauthorised accounts opened in a complainant’s name without their consent or authority, including where the unauthorised account is being used as a mule account.

A mule account is a bank account used to receive and transfer stolen funds. It can be operated by someone recruited by scammers (a money mule) or by a scammer if the account is opened by a scammer using Identification Documents as part of an ID takeover or when the account is sold or rented to the scammer.

AFCA’s jurisdiction

Yes. From 12 March 2026, AFCA can accept scam-related complaints under Rule B.2.1(j) even if the scam event occurred prior to this date. AFCA is unable to consider complaints if it falls outside AFCA’s standard time limits for lodging complaints or is not eligible under AFCA’s rules for some other reason.

No. Prior to 12 March 2026, where a complainant lodges a scam-related complaint against a bank that has not provided them with a financial service and there is no customer relationship (i.e. the receiving bank), the complaint will be outside AFCA’s Rules.

Under Rule A.4.3(a), the complaint must arise from a customer relationship or other circumstance that brings the complaint within AFCA’s jurisdiction. Rule B.2.1(a) also states that a complaint must arise in relation to the provision of a Financial Service by the Financial Firm to the Complainant.

If the complainant then lodges a duplicate complaint against the bank after 12 March 2026, that duplicate complaint will be outside of AFCA’s Rules under Rule B.2.1(a). This is because Rule A.23.5 states AFCA must apply the Rules that were in existence at the date the complaint was first submitted to AFCA.

Yes. AFCA will exclude a complaint under Rule B.2.1(j) where the complaint is lodged against an AFCA member that received scam funds, but the member is not a ‘Bank’ and has not provided a financial service or engaged in credit activities with a person who need not be the complainant.

Yes. AFCA will exclude a complaint under Rule B.2.1(j) where the complaint is lodged against a ‘Bank’, but it does not relate to an attempt to deceive the complainant.

No. Under the expanded jurisdiction, the receipt, internal movement, or external transfer of the funds must relate to an attempt to deceive the Complainant.

Mistaken internet payments are typically the result of a user accidentally entering the wrong account details, or selecting the wrong payee from a list, and do not involve an attempt to deceive.

This means AFCA will not consider complaints about a mistaken internet payment in a complaint lodged against the receiving bank unless it is performed as part of a scam. Such cases are likely to be rare.

Yes, the complaint must be lodged with AFCA for the first time against the receiving bank on or after 12 March 2026.

If the same complaint was previously lodged against the receiving bank before 12 March 2026, any duplicate complaint will be outside the rules. This includes where the complaint is lodged for the first time on 11 March 2026.AFCA will not assess jurisdiction until 13 March 2026.

No. While the expanded jurisdiction allows AFCA to consider additional scenarios involving receiving banks and the opening of unauthorised accounts, it does not change the existing exclusions under AFCA’s Rules. Complaints from businesses with more than 100 employees remain outside AFCA’s scope.

AFCA investigation process under expanded jurisdiction

Yes. AFCA considers RG 271 to be good industry practice and sets the standard for internal dispute resolution (IDR) and how a financial firm should deal with complaints, even where the complainant is not its customer.

We consider it good industry practice that before AFCA receives and investigates a complaint, the receiving bank should have completed its IDR process. If IDR has not taken place when a complaint is lodged with AFCA, AFCA will refer the complaint back to the receiving bank to complete its IDR process.

AFCA’s current processes and timeframes will apply. If the complaint has not gone through IDR, it will be referred to the bank to resolve it internally or provide its IDR response within 30 days, or within 21 days if the complainant is in financial difficulty. The case management process will remain the same.

Yes, the process will remain the same. The jurisdiction simply broadens the types of complaints we can consider.

Yes, the receiving bank can communicate with a complainant directly, and it is expected to do so to investigate and resolve their complaint.

It is up to each receiving bank to determine what other steps it takes once it is notified of a scam by a non-customer. For example, advising the non-customer to also report the scam to their own bank, or raising a scam report with the sending bank via the FRX.

As this is a new jurisdiction, we want to make sure we give each complaint due consideration on its merits to ensure we do not inappropriately exclude claims.

Under our current process, AFCA accepts all complaints at lodgement. Each complaint will be assessed on its individual merits, considering the circumstances of the complaint, the actions of the receiving bank, and any relevant documentation.

With our expanded jurisdiction, we encourage firms to train their staff on these changes to avoid unnecessary objections, particularly for receiving bank complaints.

No. AFCA will assess the issues raised against both the sending and receiving banks in separate complaints. We will use our existing processes for complaints involving receiving banks. If a complaint includes both sending and receiving banks, AFCA will investigate them together, similar to how we handle disputes against multiple financial firms.

We will not automatically join a receiving bank complaint or a sending bank complaint at the outset but may do so at a later stage if we determine it is appropriate.

If a complainant has only lodged a complaint against the sending bank, AFCA will not encourage them to also lodge a complaint against the receiving bank or create a complaint against the receiving bank, unless the complainant instructs us to do so. If we determine it is appropriate to join the receiving bank to a complaint against the sending bank, we will notify all parties.

We acknowledge the receiving banks won’t have a customer relationship with the complainant and this may cause challenges in verifying the legitimacy of their complaint. AFCA is required to accept a complaint if it meets the eligibility criteria of the expanded jurisdiction and is not otherwise excluded under our Rules. This means AFCA is unable to direct a complainant to lodge their complaint with the sending bank first.

Under AFCA’s current joinder process, a preliminary view or recommendation is only binding if all parties agree to it. If any party rejects it, both complaints must progress to a final decision (determination). A determination is binding on all parties if it’s accepted by the complainant.

Either bank may choose to resolve its individual complaint before it reaches determination by making a resolution offer to the complainant, provided the complainant accepts the offer.

No. The funding model will remain unchanged, which means fees are charged when the complaint closes depending on the stage at which it closes. It is open to each bank to resolve their individual complaint with the Complainant at any stage by making a resolution offer.

AFCA is very mindful of consistency in decision making. We peer review our decisions to ensure consistency in the future. As this is a new jurisdiction, over time we will learn the different types of claims we may see against receiving banks, and our approach to dealing with these claims may evolve over time.

AFCA has undertaken work on estimating complaint volumes. Our best indicator is the current complaint volumes we see against receiving banks which are assessed as outside our jurisdiction. Between January 2024 to February 2025, we received approximately 307 complaints against 30 banks, which is an average of about 26 complaints per month.

We have also met with FOS UK to hear about their experience with the receiving bank jurisdiction, which commenced in 2018. FOS UK informed AFCA that they haven't seen a large increase in receiving bank claims, either initially or currently.

As we do not know with any certainty the volume of complaints we will see from 12 March 2026, we will closely monitor complaint volumes and have established dedicated case management teams to handle any increased volumes.

How AFCA will assess complaints

Under the expanded jurisdiction, AFCA will be able to follow the movement of funds from the victim’s account to other banks, including intermediate and final receiving banks.

AFCA will make inquiries and request information to assess whether those receiving banks met their legal obligations or acted in a way that meets good industry practice, such as by freezing a bank account once it is on notice that the account is a mule account.

In March 2025, the Minister for Financial Services amended the conditions of AFCA’s authorisation to broaden its jurisdiction (Authorisation Condition 9B). The Authorisation Condition requires AFCA to consider complaints lodged against a ‘Bank’ if it relates to a receiving bank or unauthorised account opening. Apart from introducing these two new relationships which give rise to a complaint and a new definition of ‘Bank’, the Authorisation Condition does not authorise AFCA to amend its time limits. This means AFCA’s standard approach to complaints lodged when there is a jurisdiction change applies, and the current time limits for complaints continue to apply.

We understand industry practice has evolved over time, and the standards which apply today likely did not apply six years ago. AFCA will continue to decide complaints based on the laws, codes and industry standards that applied at the time of the events in dispute. This approach is taken across all our banking and finance complaints.

No. As part of our information gathering, AFCA will ask the bank to explain if there is any reason why it cannot destroy or de-identify the complainant’s personal information. If the bank is prohibited from destroying the complainant’s personal information under AML or other laws, it should make this submission in each individual complaint.

AFCA expects the banks to comply with their KYC obligations at law.

AFCA will likely require a copy of the receiving bank’s KYC policy applicable at the time it opened the account, to ensure it followed its policy correctly when collecting and verifying the account holder’s ID.

Where the receiving bank did not correctly follow its KYC policy, we will assess whether its actions caused the Complainant’s financial loss. If it did, we may compel the bank to compensate the Complainant.

If our investigation determines the receiving bank did not breach its KYC obligations when opening the recipient account or make any error, then it likely will not be liable for the complainant’s loss.

AFCA does not expect a receiving bank to hold funds indefinitely until it receives a response from the sending bank. Where a receiving bank releases frozen funds due to a lack of response from the sending bank, AFCA will assess all the circumstances to determine whether its action was reasonable.

Where a sending bank receives a request for information from the receiving bank, AFCA expects it to respond promptly. As this question highlights, a delay by the sending bank may adversely impact the chances of successful recovery. In this event, AFCA may find the sending bank liable for the complainant’s loss.

AFCA expects banks to meet the FRX Service Level Agreement (SLA) timeframes when acknowledging an FRX report, beginning its investigation into the report, and providing an outcome. AFCA will assess each case on its individual merits when determining whether the bank’s actions were appropriately and timely.

AFCA relies on receiving proof of a person being a scammer or fraudster. If the banks believe the person is a scammer or fraudster the bank is responsible for providing this information. Otherwise, AFCA often resolves these issues by asking the complainant to prove the source of funds. Where a person cannot provide proof, the complaint does not progress further.

Information gathering

AFCA expects to receive unredacted third-party information when it is necessary for our investigation. This is permissible under Australian Privacy Principle 6 (APP6) of the Privacy Act, provided the information is relevant and required for resolving the complaint.

Privacy obligations are important. Under APP 6.2(b) and (c), firms are permitted to share personal information without consent when required or authorised by law, including responding to AFCA’s requests where the information is necessary for our investigation.

Complete information helps AFCA resolve complaints fairly and efficiently. As privacy laws support this process, unnecessary redactions can delay outcomes for everyone involved.

AFCA will only request unredacted third-party information when necessary for our investigation. All data will be handled in accordance with AFCA’s privacy obligations, and our privacy and confidentiality policies.

At IDR, it is up to the banks to determine how much information they can share with each other while meeting their privacy and any other legal obligations.

At EDR, it is AFCA’s expectation that information be provided to us on request, and it will be handled by us in accordance with our privacy obligations.

No, the process will remain the same. Currently, AFCA does not seek third-party information directly from the receiving bank. Rather, the sending bank may request information from the receiving bank, such as redacted account statements. AFCA encourages receiving banks to continue co-operating with the sending bank’s requests for information.

Liability and compensation

Where a complaint is lodged against both the sending and receiving bank, AFCA will firstly assess whether either bank made an error or breached an obligation which caused or contributed to the complainant’s financial loss.

Liability may be apportioned where AFCA’s investigation determines the sending and receiving bank both made an error which caused or contributed to the Complainant’s financial loss. We will assess to what degree each bank’s error contributed to the loss and apportion liability on a case-by–case basis.

If the receiving bank’s investigation reveals it made an error or failed to comply with good industry practice it may be fair in all the circumstances and in accordance with community expectations, for it to make a timely offer to reimburse the loss. This promotes the efficient complaint resolution, and reduces stress and inconvenience caused to the complainant by unnecessarily protracted investigations.

To prevent complainants receiving double compensation for the same financial loss from different banks, AFCA will check our records to see whether the complainant has lodged a complaint about the same scam against another bank.

If another complaint exists, for example, against the sending bank, we will check if the complainant was fully or partially reimbursed and deduct this amount from any award of financial loss compensation, if appropriate.

If no other complaint exists, we will ask the complainant to confirm whether they have been compensated for their financial loss by another financial firm and may request copies of any complaint outcome letters between the complainant and their bank, or consent to speak with their bank.

If the receiving bank determines there is a competing interest to the funds available in the recipient account, it should make submissions to AFCA on this matter. AFCA will consider the circumstances on a case-by-case basis.

Timing and funding

The proposed AFCA Rule changes will take effect on and from 12 March 2026.

No, AFCA’s existing funding model will apply. AFCA will charge members a fee when it closes the complaint depending upon the stage at which the complaint closes.

Support available

AFCA will engage members through upcoming information sessions and other resources to help banks understand and navigate the complaint process. AFCA will send out more information on these and update the website over the coming months.

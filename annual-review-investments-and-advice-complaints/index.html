Key complaint trends

More investment and advice complaints

Investment and advice complaints were up 18% from 2023‑24 (3,559) and mostly related to:

There is a clear link between a financial firm’s business model and complaint volumes. Recent high‑profile product collapses, which include the Shield Master Fund and the First Guardian Master Fund continue to drive this trend.

  • complaints with concerns about overall business models including cold calling
  • and pressured sales tactics
  • conflicted advice
  • undiversified and common product recommendations to the majority of a financial firm’s clients.

Failure to act in client’s best interest becomes the most common issue

Of the 4,193 complaints we received, 1,266 or over 30% were complaints about failure to act in client’s best interests. In contrast, complaints about inappropriate advice fell by 44%. This is despite the usual overlap between these two categories; the drop was primarily due to AFCA considering more Compensation Scheme of Last Resort (CSLR) cases where advisers failed to act in the client’s best interests.

SMSF and share complaints drive up volume

Out of the total number of complaints, 32% (1,323) and 28% (1,168) were about SMSFs and shares respectively. This is again a reflection of business models which involve the establishment of SMSFs and investment in products related to the financial firm.

Complaints related to unlisted shares and representations about listings and future performances also grew. These were mostly about recommendations to buy unlisted shares in a company planning to list with the promise of short to medium‑term growth.

Superannuation wrap platform and managed investment schemes (MIS) complaints on the rise

As with many SMSF complaints, these often involve recommendations to rollover superannuation into a ‘wrap’ platform that concentrates investments into a single fund. The spike in complaints includes those mostly brought against solvent financial firms that were impacted by the Shield Master Fund and the First Guardian Fund collapses.

Resolution and timeframes

AFCA closed 4,137 investment and advice complaints in 2024‑25, of which 21% were closed at Referral stage.

Complaints closed at Rules Review stage falls

We closed a third of complaints at the Rules Review stage, 25% fewer than in 2023‑24. The decline is due to the 2023‑24 review of paused complaints for CSLR eligibility, with out‑of‑scope cases adding to the higher closure numbers.

In contrast, AFCA closed 18% more complaints at the Case Management stage and 686 cases were closed at Decision stage (up from 280 in the previous year). It was encouraging to see less need for AFCA determinations. The increase at the Decisions stage is largely due to AFCA now determining insolvent financial firm complaints after the establishment of the CSLR, noting that these complaints are not capable of early resolution due to non‑responsive insolvent financial firms.

Industry trends and challenges

Compensation Scheme of Last Resort

In its first year of operation, the CSLR accounted for 70% of AFCA’s investment and advice decisions, a proportion that will continue to grow, as more CSLR complaints are resolved.

AFCA focused on finalising complaints that had been paused while awaiting the establishment of the scheme. All were completed except those involving Dixon Advisory, which remains the largest batch of complaints AFCA has ever received. While many Dixon Advisory matters were resolved in 2024‑25, a significant number remain in progress.

AFCA found in favour of 97% of complainants eligible for CSLR and awarded compensation of $117 million.

Inadequate and conflicted financial advice models are a key driver of more decisions against insolvent financial firms and we are seeing more of this in high‑profile failures. Addressing these conflicted business models requires an industry‑wide response to reform the sector.

Complaints against insolvent investment and advice financial firms continued to grow, making 2024‑25 the second‑highest year on record, following the peak in 2022‑23 driven by the collapse of Dixon Advisory.

To meet this growing demand, AFCA has expanded its workforce and streamlined its services. For complaints involving insolvent financial firms, we have worked closely with insolvency practitioners to obtain the information needed, reducing the burden on consumers.

Other complaints

Complaints about other areas continued, including:

  • contracts for different complaints, predominantly about pricing and margin close outs
  • share complaints, predominately about unlisted shares and to a lesser extent the operation of managed discretionary accounts
  • risk advice, predominately in relation to appropriate levels of cover relative to premium costs
  • timeshare MIS, predominantly about the promotion of the schemes and compliance with the scheme’s constitution
  • MIS, particularly those related to the property sector which targeted above market returns
  • personal advice to invest in superannuation wrap platforms and MIS.

Case study

High profile advice/fund collapse complaint

Background

This complaint involved two insolvent financial firms. Firm one provided the initial advice and Firm two the subsequent advice.

According to the complainant, Firm one repeatedly called wanting him to compare his existing industry super fund to alternative options, despite his insistence that he was happy with his existing super. Eventually however, he responded to Firm ones insistence that his super was being eroded by high fees.

In 2022, Firm one advised him to roll his super into an SMSF and invest in Funds S and P. In 2024, Firm two advised the SMSF to divest those investments.

Complaint

The complainant alleged:

Firm one’s advice was inappropriate because it unnecessarily increased risks associated with his superannuation.

Firm two failed to provide timely advice to divest Funds S and P, and failed to act on Mr S’ instructions to redeem those investments.

Outcome

AFCA found Firm one’s advice to Mr S was inappropriate because:

The complainant suffered direct financial losses.

Firm two breached its duty of care by failing to promptly carry out the SMSF trustee’s instructions to redeem its Fund P investment, despite the fund still being open for redemptions. This delay meant the redemption was not processed before the fund closed in 2024 further contributing to the complainant’s losses.

Firm one was made to pay $181,402.88 compensation, plus $5,000 towards winding up the SMSF.

Firm two was made to pay $36,409.55.

Interest was applied to both amounts until payment, with payments reduced by any distributions or funds recovered from the external administrator of the financial firms and/or from Fund S and Fund P.

  • Mr S had limited time and experience to act as the trustee of an SMSF, and the increased compliance and associated costs of APRA regulated funds without the material benefit.
  • Financial Firm one’s advice to exit a well‑diversified portfolio in Fund S – a MIS lending to real estate developments – lacked diversification and could not reasonably be expected to improve his wealth generation prospects.

Case study

Representations about capital raising for unlisted securities

Background

The complainant purchased shares in a Capital Raising Equity offer after discussions with the financial firm’s representatives. The shares were in the financial firm’s parent company.

Complaint

According to the complainant, the financial firm led him to believe that the parent company would list on the Australian Securities Exchange (ASX) within three to six months and a separate agreement would underpin the value of the shares at listing. As the parent company did not ultimately list on the ASX the complainant felt misled.

Outcome

An AFCA panel found the financial firm had not misled the complainant, as he qualified as a sophisticated investor under section 708 of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth). This meant the firm faced lower disclosure obligations (for example, a prospectus) than for a retail clien

The panel also found no evidence of wrongdoing because:

The AFCA panel found it would not be fair to require the financial firm to pay compensation to the complainant because he had not evidenced the financial firm engaged in any wrongdoing.

  • the statements in the Information Memorandum were not made by the financial firm
  • the complainant could not substantiate that the financial firm’s representatives made additional statements about a listing occurring within three to six months.

Case study

Negotiated outcome on TPD claim advice

Background

The complaint concerned financial advice given between 2009 and 2020. The complainant ceased work in 2014 due to ongoing health issues and was on income protection benefits.

In 2020, a new adviser reviewed his insurance and recommended a TPD claim. It was successful and the complainant received more than $1,400,000.

Complaint

The complainant questioned why he was not advised to claim earlier. He argued if he had claimed when he ceased work in 2014, then between 2014 and 2020 he would have earned over $670,000 in interest.

Outcome

AFCA reviewed over 600 documents, which included medical reports. The available information suggested that by 2017, the previous adviser could have been aware of this as an option and may have had an opportunity to advised him to lodge a TPD claim.

AFCA facilitated discussions, which led to a settlement. Both parties thanked AFCA for resolving what had been a long and complex dispute.

This is a good example of how AFCA can help parties reach a fair, efficient and negotiated resolution.

¹ This excludes complaints that were inactive for an extended period. For example, complaints that were paused because the financial firm was insolvent or due to court proceedings, and complaints that were previously closed and then re‑opened.

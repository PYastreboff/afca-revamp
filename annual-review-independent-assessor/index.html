About the Independent Assessor

Melissa Dwyer is AFCA’s Independent Assessor (IA). She was appointed by, and reports directly to AFCA’s Board, operating within the IA’s Terms of Reference, and investigates and assesses concerns about how AFCA handles complaints against service delivery standards for process fairness.

Importantly, under the Terms of Reference, the IA is not authorised to review the merits of a decision which includes determinations and jurisdictional decisions. The role is to evaluate the fairness of AFCA’s processes.

The IA offers valuable feedback and recommendations to improve our complaint‑handling procedures, but does not participate in AFCA’s daily operations.

Overview

The IA’s review for 2024‑25 highlighted notable advancements, together with opportunities for improvement at AFCA.

Independent Assessor complaints in 2024‑25

In 2024‑25, the IA received 342 complaints, which was a significant increase over the past two financial years (2023‑24: 288; 2022‑23: 257), but still only equates to 0.33% of all complaints. The top five issues were:

  • inadequate responses to correspondence and/or calls
  • delays in handling a financial firm complaint
  • lack of procedural fairness
  • bias
  • failure to comply with Rules, Operational Guidelines and other policies.

Of the 342 complaints received, 77 assessments were issued. Of those 77 assessments, 40 included at least one substantiated complaint.

The IA top four substantiated issues were related to:

  • efficiency – unreasonable delays in handling a financial firm complaint
  • communication – inadequate responses to correspondence and/or calls
  • communication – providing poor quality information or advice
  • effectiveness – not following correct process.

Recommendations

When a complaint is substantiated, the IA can recommend to AFCA’s Chief Ombudsman that we:

During 2024‑25, the IA recommended 39 apologies to complainants for service failings together with $17,450 in non‑financial loss compensation. On seven occasions, the IA also recommended increasing non‑financial loss compensation for failings AFCA had already acknowledged and apologised for.

The IA also recommended we:

AFCA fully accepted and actioned all of IA’s recommendations.

  • offer an apology
  • pay compensation for any distress or inconvenience caused (non‑financial loss)
  • take other action.
  • more clearly explain why we have closed a complaint, which includes what we did or could have done in order to get an apology from the financial firm, if the complainant wanted this to resolve the complaint
  • explain why we could not help with the remaining concerns about a financial firm.

Observations

Communication

The most substantiated problem highlighted was inadequate responses to complainant calls, correspondence and specific questions. Examples included:

  • not sending them a financial firm’s submission when requested
  • not giving regular updates on their complaint, even after they followed up
  • not explaining why we could not help with certain concerns
  • ignoring requests to prioritise a complaint.

Efficiency and effectiveness

Delays in handling a financial firm complaint, together with the lack of progress updates on a complaint, were the most common efficiency‑related complaints substantiated during 2024‑25. AFCA is taking robust and coordinated action to reduce both queues and wait times, and significant progress has been made. The IA is continuing to closely monitor delays, and AFCA’s communication and management of them.

Other complaints involved process failures such as wrongly merging two complaints and not redacting ‘sensitive’ information in accordance with AFCA’s internal policy.

Fairness and impartiality

The IA substantiated seven complaints about the lack of procedural fairness. Most of these involved AFCA not providing complainants with a reasonable opportunity to make submissions before an outcome was issued.

In one case that the IA substantiated, a manager who had reviewed a complainant’s objections to an initial jurisdictional assessment had also quality assured the original decision.

A few other complaints alleged bias and conflict of interest, but none were substantiated.

Accessibility

During 2024‑25, the IA received 21 complaints about accessibility.

These included:

No complaints about the accessibility of AFCA’s service were substantiated.

  • ten allegations of disability discrimination
  • six complaints that AFCA had not used a complainant’s advised preferred communication method
  • five complaints that AFCA had not adequately responded to a complainant’s accessibility needs.

Business improvement recommendations

Under Clause 3 of the IA’s Terms of Reference the IA can recommend ways for AFCA to improve its response to service complaint issues.

During 2024‑25, the IA recommended that AFCA:

Business improvement recommendations, together with AFCA’s responses, are reported to AFCA’s Board which monitors their implementation.

As at 30 June 2025, three of the five recommendations were fully addressed.

  • Consider updating its ‘Threat to Self‑Harm or Others procedure’ to ensure imminent risk is clearly defined and to provide guidance on how to assess immediacy during threat assessments.
  • Consider providing non‑financial firm death‑benefit dispute parties with clear written information about confidentiality requirements and AFCA’s approach to information exchange and redactions.
  • Review the resourcing of its privacy function to ensure privacy‑related requests are being processed accurately and on a timely basis
  • Conduct an end‑to‑end review of its process for hard‑copy document delivery to ensure AFCA can verify, in line with its document retention policy, that complainants receive documents sent by post
  • Ensure its approach to the requirement for authorisation by representatives of complainant companies is fit‑for‑purpose and consistently applied.

Case study

Complainants experiencing vulnerability and complex behaviours

Background

The IA receives many complaints from people experiencing vulnerability, which sometimes involves dealing with complex behaviours. These can present unique risks and need to be handled with care and sensitivity. The IA observed a small number of instances where complainants experiencing vulnerability were not adequately supported by AFCA. One complainant repeatedly told AFCA that his complaint had been misunderstood . AFCA did not sufficiently respond, follow‑up or adequately address these concerns and missed the opportunity to resolve the issue sooner, and minimise distress.

Another complaint involved ambiguity in the determination about the scope of vehicle damage to be repaired, which caused considerable confusion for a complainant experiencing vulnerability. AFCA’s responses to requests for clarification and assistance were inadequate; therefore, the complainant lodged a further complaint about the same issue and experienced additional distress.

Findings and outcome

In 2023‑24, the IA recommended AFCA improve how it identifies and manages complainants experiencing vulnerability and complex behaviours. This continues remains a focus of the IA’s reports to AFCA’s Board.

AFCA has since taken action to rectify this, which includes:

  • Previous section - Feedback about our service
  • Next section - Corporate governance statement
  • Establishing hypercare teams within Case Management to use internal expertise and help caseworkers to better manage these complaints from complainants experiencing vulnerability and/or those with complex needs or behaviours.
  • Launching the Equity of Access Program, a strategic initiative aligned with AFCA’s customer experience strategy. This program aims to make AFCA’s service more accessible and adaptable to individual needs, recognising that equity requires personalised pathways rather than a one‑size‑fits‑all approach.

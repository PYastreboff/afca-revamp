Key complaint trends

Another spike in general insurance complaints this year

In 2024‑25, AFCA received 34,231 general insurance complaints, a 17% increase from 2023‑24, mainly caused by a surge of complaints about add‑on insurance. This contrasts with an overall decrease of 4% across all product areas. High complaint volumes persistently highlight the need for industry to do more to prevent avoidable complaints reaching AFCA, particularly where claim delays are the issue.

Motor vehicle insurance led complaint numbers; however add‑on insurance complaints grew significantly

Comprehensive motor vehicle insurance was again the most complained about insurance product, making up a third of all cases. Delays are increasingly driven by industry‑wide shortages in vehicle parts and skilled labour, including the ever‑expanding electric vehicle market. To maintain trust, insurers must address reasons for delays, review product offerings, and effectively communicate challenges transparently and proactively, so that customers are clear about why, and for how long, they must wait.

Add‑on insurance

During 2024‑25, we received 7,880 add‑on insurance complaints (23%) which represented nearly a quarter of all general insurance complaints. These products – such as consumer credit insurance, guaranteed asset protection, tyre and rim, and extended warranties – have often been sold alongside loans for vehicles. In 2024‑25, we resolved more than 4,500 of these complaints, helping to deliver better outcomes for affected consumers.

Complaints commonly raised concerns about unfair sales practices, poor product design, inadequate disclosure and instances of misrepresentation or undue pressure. While some financial firms implemented remediation programs, many consumers raised complaints with AFCA. Most were resolved through agreement or recommendation, and 319 were resolved through formal Determination. Nearly three‑quarters of complaints were against nine members, mainly large banks.

To support fair outcomes, AFCA worked closely with financial firms, complainants and their representatives, to encourage early resolution and ensure clarity about our approach. We also identified possible systemic issues, reporting concerns to regulators about misleading practices, breaches of advice obligations, poor complaint handling and residual harm not addressed through earlier remediation. Updated resources, including our Add‑on Insurance Fact Sheet and Response Guide, provided further clarity to both firms and consumers.

Misleading product/service information became the top issue, driven by add‑on insurance complaints

In 2024‑25, AFCA received 7,515 complaints about misleading product/service information (22% of all general insurance complaints) – up 365% from 2023‑24. This was driven by complaints about add‑on insurance and related disputed issues.

Resolution and timeframes

Complaint closures continue to increase year‑on‑year

AFCA closed 31,432 general insurance complaints in 2024‑25, a 10% increase from 2023‑24.

Nearly half were resolved at the earliest stages of AFCA’s process

We resolved 45% of general insurance complaints at the Referral stage, up 9%. We also closed more complaints at Case Management stage (11,516 compared to 10,352).

The proportion of complaints closed at Decision stage is reducing. This is encouraging and we continue to urge financial firms to resolve complaints as early in the process as possible, reducing the effects of drawn‑out complaints on both complainants and financial firms.

Complaints closed at the Rules Review stage grew by 28%

The Rules Review stage saw a 28% increase in complaint closures – 3,228 closed.

More than half were closed within 60 days

More than half of all closed general insurance complaints were resolved within 60 days, with 8,723 closed in 30 days. Only 4% took more than 365 days to resolve.

Industry trends and challenges

Motor vehicle claim delays make up one in four of all general insurance complaints referred to AFCA and are a longstanding pain point for customers. Many people complain directly to us rather than via their insurer’s IDR process. We know that public trust in the sector has become increasingly strained by these delays.

Unresolved cases or unexplained delays lead to customer stress, anxiety and physical inconvenience. It can result in AFCA awarding non‑financial loss compensation. The spotlight is on the industry to focus on resolving the complaints, but also to fix the underlying issues causing dissatisfaction. We have been working with industry, sharing our insights and data to support positive change in financial firms.

Ongoing harm also arises from drawn out, or poorly handled home insurance claims. We are concerned about delays in making claims decisions, undertaking assessments and repairs, and providing temporary accommodation. Sometimes insurers seek to cash settle when they should be finishing repairs. We also see insurers not recognising or responding appropriately to complainants who experience vulnerability – this conduct is inconsistent with the industry’s code of practice.

Case study

Insurer’s delay in claim handling caused significant stress

Background

The complainant lodged an insurance claim after their house was damaged by leaking water.

Complaint

The insurer took a year to accept the claim. There were no documented reasons to explain why the decision took so long. There were then significant delays in doing the repairs, with mould containment and decontamination completed 18 months after the claim was lodged. Only then could repairs start. The complainant was out of their house for nearly two years while the claim was being processed.

Outcome

Even with the insurer’s payment for accommodation above policy limits, the ombudsman awarded the maximum $6,300 in non‑financial loss. This was due to the complainant’s stress and loss of enjoyment caused by the claim handling.

Case study

Insurer’s decision to cash settle outstanding damage not fair or reasonable

Background

After storm damage to her property, the complainant made a successful claim under her home and contents policy. Repairs began almost two years later. The insurer then said the relationship between its trades and the complainant had broken down and sought to cash settle the outstanding work.

Complaint

The complainant wanted more mould remediation done and all repairs finished by the insurer.

Outcome

The ombudsman found that it was not fair or reasonable for the insurer to cash settle the outstanding repairs after starting the work and that the remaining repairs must be completed; there was no compelling reason for the insurer not to complete the works. The complainant’s family was experiencing vulnerability and further delays were likely if she had to organise the remaining work.

Some delays were contributed to by the complainant, but this was due to their concerns about wanting the mould problem dealt with before the building repairs so that they had a long‑term solution. They also had to find temporary housing while the work was being done.

The insurer caused significant delays. Poor communication by the insurer meant the complainant was unsure about what was happening. Despite multiple calls for an update by the complainant, the insurer took no action to progress the claim. The insurer also failed to properly address the family’s vulnerability, even though the complainant raised it repeatedly.

Almost two years after the claim was lodged, the insurer allocated the claim to a specialist case manager due to the family’s vulnerability. The complainant disclosed that their daughter had been hospitalised due to the mould.

The ombudsman found the insurer had mishandled the claim. The insurer had to reimburse the complainant for the mould contamination report they provided, remediate the mould damage, pay the family’s moving costs and accommodation, review the contents claim and finish the repairs. The insurer also had to pay $6,300 compensation for non‑financial loss.

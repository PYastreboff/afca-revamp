Expanding Jurisdiction: Building on trust and capability

AFCA’s expanding jurisdictions reflect the confidence that Government, regulators, industry and the community have placed in AFCA’s ability to deliver fair, independent and effective dispute resolution. Over the past…

Annual Review 2024-25

Contents

AFCA’s expanding jurisdictions reflect the confidence that Government, regulators, industry and the community have placed in AFCA’s ability to deliver fair, independent and effective dispute resolution. Over the past seven years, AFCA has demonstrated that we can manage complex disputes at scale, adapt to changing financial markets, and provide trusted outcomes for consumers and financial firms alike.

From scams and digital assets to Buy Now Pay Later products and beyond, AFCA’s remit needs to change in step with the evolving financial landscape, to ensure fair outcomes for both consumers, and support for members, both existing and new.

As we take on these new areas, we remain focused on ensuring Australians have the confidence that when harm occurs in financial services, there is a trusted place to turn to for complaint resolution and redress. We will continue to work closely with Government, regulators, industry and consumer groups to support the effective design and implementation of these new jurisdictions.

National Scams Prevention Framework

The human cost of scams

Scams continue to cause devastating personal and financial outcomes, often wiping out life savings, damaging mental health, destroying small businesses and devastating lives. Scammers exploit gaps in systems, technology and regulations. During 2024‑25, AFCA received 5,977 scam complaints, which represents just a small proportion of the total number of scams people experience. According to ASIC, during 2024‑25, financial firms received 159,000+ scam and fraud complaints.

Reform to meet a growing challenge

Current legislative and regulatory settings are not fit‑for‑purpose and do not prevent the scale, complexity and sophistication of today’s scams. The landmark Scams Prevention Framework (SPF), passed by Parliament in February 2025, seeks to address this. AFCA welcomed the opportunity to contribute our insights to Government and parliamentary processes, as part of the Senate Economics Legislation Committee’s assessment of the provisions of the SPF Bill in January 2025. We highlighted gaps in current laws and codes and advocated for a cross‑sector approach. We are committed to the smooth and effective implementation of reforms.

The SPF will establish clear obligations for banks, telecommunications providers and digital platforms to prevent, detect, disrupt, report and respond to scams. It has also set up the first national EDR jurisdiction for scam complaints. The Government has expressed its intention to designate this role to AFCA.

AFCA’s preparation and expertise

In preparing for this responsibility, AFCA has established a dedicated program of work that draws on our extensive experience in resolving 27,000+ scam complaints over the past five years. We are providing our insights and experience of dealing with scam complaints to support a framework that ensures early and effective prevention and remediation for scam victims, together with a simple, fair and accessible pathway for redress when harm occurs. We are contributing to the development of rules, codes and operating models to make the framework a success.

AFCA has new powers from March 2026 to consider complaints involving ‘receiving banks’. This is typically where stolen funds are transferred into and out of ‘mule accounts’ used by scammers. This change permits AFCA to consider the actions of receiving banks as part of the full chain of events in a scam or other similar thefts. AFCA will also gain the ability to consider accounts opened at the receiving bank using stolen identification documents. This reform addresses a gap by increasing the accountability of receiving banks and the transparency in the movement of the scammed funds.

Collaboration across sectors on resolving scam disputes

Taking on scams dispute resolution across multiple sectors will require a new and collaborative approach. Typically, financial disputes involve both a consumer and financial firm, but scams can involve several parties across different sectors. That is why clear rules and codes with shared responsibility are essential for timely and fair outcomes. AFCA will seek to resolve these disputes fairly, efficiently and at scale, and share insights to strengthen prevention and reduce harm.

Working with industry, Government and advocates

The success of the SPF will rely on the Government’s current design work, and AFCA is already engaging closely with stakeholders to strengthen relationships and deepen our understanding of the unique challenges each industry faces. These are fastmoving, complex issues that will require each sector’s deep expertise and input into developing workable, successful rules and codes. AFCA’s role is to make sure redress is fair, timely and consistent across industries. The commencement date for the new jurisdiction will be advised by Government.

Looking ahead

The SPF underscores the importance of shared responsibility and collaboration across sectors. Success will depend on clear obligations and consequences under the codes, and an easy to apply liability framework that will support early resolution via an internal dispute resolution (IDR), the knowledge and capabilities of regulators and Government, and the participation of telecommunications providers, digital platforms, banks and consumer advocates to ensure a fit‑for‑purpose framework is implemented. Together we can create a practical, effective and resilient framework and a new benchmark for how Australia prevents scams, adapts to emerging threats and supports victims.

Digital assets (cryptocurrency)

Investment in cryptocurrency and other digital assets has grown rapidly, with around one in four Australians now owning cryptocurrency. Serious consumer risk is arising from market volatility, misleading promotions, exchange collapses and a rise in crypto‑related scams.

AFCA welcomes the Australian Government’s move to bring greater clarity and accountability to this sector by applying financial services laws to digital assets. These reforms ensure alignment with the broader financial services framework to better protect consumers, bolster market integrity and ensure AFCA’s access without delay.

To support the transition, in December 2024, ASIC released Consultation Paper 381. This paper proposed updates to Information Sheet 225 on cryptocurrency assets and financial services and gave examples of when a digital asset would be considered a financial product. It suggested transitional relief for providers applying for a licence.

AFCA is experienced in dealing with complaints relating to cryptocurrency. AFCA currently has voluntary members in the cryptocurrency sector, and we expect to see a significant increase in members as a result of the changes to Information Sheet 225.

AFCA has a dedicated project team working with our new cryptocurrency members so they can meet their compliance obligations, strengthen their IDR processes and embed good practice from day one. We will draw on our experience supporting new and emerging financial service providers like Low‑Cost Credit Contracts (LCCC), including BNPL products and fintech firms. We are working closely with regulators, consumer groups and industry to ensure a smooth transition.

Other jurisdictions, such as the United Kingdom and European Union, have introduced bespoke frameworks. We welcome the Australian regulators’ approach of using the existing regime. We believe it will offer a faster pathway for consumers seeking access to complaints resolution and redress.

We expect to see crypto‑related complaints grow as the sector transitions into the regulated environment. We will focus on resolving these disputes fairly, efficiently and as early as possible – including disputes involving scams, which are increasingly linked to digital asset platforms.

Buy Now Pay Later and related reforms

A new jurisdiction established for AFCA, as part of strengthened protection for Buy Now Pay Later (BNPL) and low‑cost credit contract (LCCC) consumers.

LCCCs, including BNPL products, are experiencing rapid growth, particularly among younger Australians. AFCA has dealt with BNPL complaints for many years, with much of the industry choosing to be voluntary AFCA members.

From 10 June 2025, LCCCs, including BNPL products, have been regulated under the National Consumer Credit Protection Act 2009 (Cth). This means responsible lending laws – or a modified version – apply. LCCCs providers must hold an Australian Credit Licence and be AFCA members. As BNPL products are now treated like other forms of consumer credit, consumers have greater access to AFCA’s independent dispute resolution service. We support these changes and have engaged closely with Government, regulators, consumer groups and industry to help ensure a smooth transition to complaints handled under the new legislative and regulatory framework. We drew on our experience with voluntary BNPL members to onboard new ones, assist the sector to build capability and help improve complaint‑handling standards across the industry.

We are watching the sector with interest, to see whether complaint volumes grow or new LCCC complaint types arise, for example, responsible lending, credit reporting and complaints related to our members’ new credit licence obligations.

We caution financial firms to manage complaints and escalations carefully, being particularly mindful of how they respond to customers requesting help with financial difficulty where they may be experiencing vulnerability. AFCA is committed to fair outcomes for all Australian LCCC consumers and industry members.

  • better support for customers in hardship
  • credit reporting obligations
  • stronger internal complaint‑handling standards.

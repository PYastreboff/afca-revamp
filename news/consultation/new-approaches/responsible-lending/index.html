Responsible Lending Approach Consultation summary

Discover AFCA's stance on responsible lending practices, aimed at protecting consumers and ensuring fair lending standards in the financial industry.

From 31 July to 11 September 2023, AFCA invited members, consumer groups, industry bodies and other stakeholders to submit feedback and questions during consultation on its draft Approach to Responsible Lending.

This page provides a summary of the consultation, the feedback and questions raised and AFCA’s response.

About Responsible Lending

Responsible Lending complaints are about consumer credit provided for personal, domestic or household purposes, such as personal loans, credit cards, and home loans. The Responsible Lending Approach explains how we consider these complaints, including:

  • how we assess a financial firm’s compliance with responsible lending obligations
  • how we determine a fair outcome where a firm breaches its responsible lending obligations, and
  • how we calculate loss and assess benefits to determine compensation.

Updates to the Responsible Lending Approach – May 2025

In February 2025, AFCA commenced a check-in consultation with financial firms and consumer groups about how the Responsible Lending Approach is operating in practice. We received written and verbal feedback which generally covered five key themes. You can read a summary of the themes and overview of key changes below. You can read the updated Approach here.(opens in new tab)

Key feedback themes

We acknowledge this feedback and have been doing significant work across our teams to embed the Approach and provide additional support to our people. We will continue this work.

We have made some changes to the wording of the Approach to clarify some of these queries and clean up some language.

We have reviewed the existing wording of the Approach in light of the feedback and in some cases we believe the existing wording is sufficient. For example, on page 41 we outline when and how we will apply interest to compensation amounts. On pages 43-44, we discuss how we determine the appropriate interest to apply to adjusted debts for secured loans where a complainant retains an asset. We have considered whether deposits should continue to generally form part of complainants’ gross loss, and we are satisfied they should, as outlined in the tables in Guide One.

We have made some changes to the wording of the Approach to clarify some of these queries, and provided further guidance about how lenders may resolve inconsistency between benchmarks, declared expense amounts and verification material . We have also considered the guidance about interest rate buffers on page 25, and we have made a small clarification that APRA guidance applies to residential mortgage loans, not just residential home loans. We are comfortable the remainder of that section accurately reflects our approach to interest rate buffers on new and existing variable rate loans.

Stakeholders expressed a desire for more issues to be covered in the Approach, including unjust transactions, pre-NCCP Act loans and broker conduct.

We understand why consumer groups and financial firms would like to see us expand the scope of the Approach, but we are also mindful of feedback we received that the Approach is already lengthy. At this stage, we have not included additional guidance in the Approach to Responsible Lending. These matters are important, however, and we will explore ways to provide guidance to parties about how AFCA will approach them.

  • The Approach has enabled early settlements and resolutions Financial firms and consumer advocates told us the Approach helped them understand what the outcome of an AFCA complaint about responsible lending complaints is likely to be, which has allowed them to proactively resolve complaints. In some instances, it has helped complaints resolve in IDR without needing to come to AFCA. Other complaints have resolved earlier in the AFCA process without needing a final decision because the parties have clarity about the approach AFCA will take.
  • Applying the Approach consistently to complaints Stakeholders told us that there is still work to do at AFCA to ensure the Approach is applied consistently throughout our process. In particular, clearly communicating to the parties how we are applying the Approach to the facts of their complaint.
  • Clarification of some loss calculation concepts Stakeholders asked for additional clarity about some matters set out in the loss calculation section of the Approach. Stakeholders requested more guidance about: how we determine the interest rate when applying interest to secured adjusted debts when we would apply interest to compensation amounts when we consider acquisition, sale and holding costs form part of a consumer’s net loss whether deposits paid are considered part of capital loss, and when crystallised capital gains are considered a benefit.
  • how we determine the interest rate when applying interest to secured adjusted debts
  • when we would apply interest to compensation amounts
  • when we consider acquisition, sale and holding costs form part of a consumer’s net loss
  • whether deposits paid are considered part of capital loss, and
  • when crystallised capital gains are considered a benefit.
  • Providing more definitive guidance about common serviceability assessment queries Stakeholders requested more detail about how AFCA considers some issues affecting serviceability assessments and unsuitability assessments, including: verification of a consumer’s living expenses where declared expenses are above a benchmark but below the amount shown in verification information what steps financial firms should take to ensure pre-existing loans are closed out where their assessment that a loan is not unsuitable relies on those loans being closed, and interest rate buffers for variable interest rate loans that are not residential mortgage loans, or for non-ADI variable interest rate residential mortgage loans
  • verification of a consumer’s living expenses where declared expenses are above a benchmark but below the amount shown in verification information
  • what steps financial firms should take to ensure pre-existing loans are closed out where their assessment that a loan is not unsuitable relies on those loans being closed, and
  • interest rate buffers for variable interest rate loans that are not residential mortgage loans, or for non-ADI variable interest rate residential mortgage loans
  • Expanding the scope of the Approach to cover more issues

Summary of changes

We made several changes to the Approach based on the feedback we received in the check-in consultation:

We hope these amendments will make the Approach clearer and more useful for financial firms and complainants.

  • Page 6 – Amended language to reflect passage of the new Low-Cost Credit Contracts regulatory regime, including the new ASIC Regulatory Guide 281.
  • Page 22 – Included a new summary paragraph about how lenders may resolve inconsistency between benchmarks, declared expense amounts, and verification material.
  • Page 25 – Clarified that APRA guidance on interest rate buffers applies to “residential mortgage loans”.
  • Page 37 – Updated loss calculation table with examples to clarify meaning.
  • Page 42 – Clarified when we may consider holding costs, acquisition costs and sale costs as part of a consumer’s gross loss.
  • Page 53 – Clarified language about capital gain and capital loss for home loans.

Consultation response

Consultation feedback summary

Following feedback, we have provided greater clarity in many areas including:

  • AFCA’s Consultation Feedback Report(opens in new tab)
  • the role of technology and innovation in credit assessment methods that comply with the law
  • factors affecting the level of inquiries and verification steps that are reasonable in any given complaint
  • how we will consider a financial firm’s assessment of reasonably foreseeable changes in a complainant’s financial circumstances,
  • how we review whether a financial firm reasonably assessed a credit product was likely to meet a complainant’s requirements and objectives, and
  • repayment of adjusted debts and applicable interest rates.

Verification requirements and use of benchmarks

Consultation feedback

  • Industry stakeholders requested changes to the draft Approach including further illustration of the ‘scalability’ of reasonable inquiries and verification steps.
  • Consumer groups asked for clarity, including about whether using benchmarks alone is sufficient to meet the required standard of reasonable verification.

AFCA response

In response to this feedback, we have added:

  • new separate sections about circumstances where we may consider more or fewer verification steps may be reasonable
  • a case example where AFCA considered fewer verification steps were reasonable, and
  • a new section titled ‘Undertakings to reduce existing expenses’.

Changes the financial firm could reasonably have foreseen

Consultation feedback

  • A key theme for stakeholders was how this section aligns with ASIC Regulatory Guide 209 (RG 209).
  • Stakeholders also asked AFCA to clarify what we may consider reasonable when assessing whether a consumer will be able to repay a loan in retirement.

AFCA response

We have improved this section in a number of ways, including:

  • adding more information about our expectation where a complainant is closer or further from retirement when applying for a loan, and
  • clarifying our expectations about reasonable use of interest rate buffers.

Assessing requirements and objectives

Consultation feedback

A number of stakeholders requested that we include information about how we consider if a financial firm has reasonably assessed if a credit contract meets a complainant’s requirements and objectives.

AFCA response

In response, we have added a new section explaining how we assess if a contract meets a complainant’s requirements and objectives and examples of common issues.

How we determine fair outcomes and calculate a complainant's loss

Consultation feedback

Stakeholders requested AFCA provide more detail about how we calculate loss and benefits in circumstances where we determine a loan is unsuitable.

  • Stakeholders requested AFCA amend how we propose to calculate loss and benefit in circumstances where we determined a loan is unsuitable.
  • Stakeholders also asked for more details about how an adjusted debt should fairly be repaid.

AFCA response

  • We have maintained a principles-based approach that aligns with law and regulatory guidance, while maintaining flexibility to do what is fair in the circumstances of particular complaints.
  • We have clarified the principles we apply to assess loss and determine fair outcomes, including more information about when we may determine how interest should be paid on an adjusted debt.

Serviceability Assessment Tool

Consultation feedback

Industry stakeholders requested clarity about how AFCA intended financial firms would use the tool.

AFCA response

The tool will be optional only.

Thank you and more information

  • The final Responsible Lending Approach has now been published.

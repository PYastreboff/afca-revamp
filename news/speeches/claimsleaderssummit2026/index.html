Claims Leaders Summit 2026

Good morning everyone, and welcome to this year’s Claims Leader’s Summit.

Welcome and introduction

Good morning everyone, and welcome to this year’s Claims Leader’s Summit.

My name is Deborah Jenkins, I’m the inaugural Chief Customer Officer at the Australian Financial Complaints Authority, and I’m really pleased to be here with you this morning to open the summit. As Chief Customer Officer, I’m responsible for all of our frontline teams – our customer service area, our case management and the customer experience for all customers – members and consumers.

Before I begin, I’d like to acknowledge the Traditional Custodians of the land we’re meeting on today, the Gadigal people of the Eora Nation, and pay my respects to their Elders past and present. I extend that respect to any Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people joining us today. I’ve just spent last week up in Gimuy and the lands of the Gimuy Walubara Yidinji peoples at the Financial Counsellors Australia conference and a really important aspect of my time there was hearing firsthand First Nations peoples experience of the financial system – a timely reminder to me of the work that we have all got to do to ensure that our services are designed to be inclusive and take into account lived experiences of those using them. This really links to the theme of my speech today.

I’ve been in this role at AFCA for coming up to 3 months now, and I wanted to begin today by sharing some reflections from that time.

Not prescriptions. Not instructions. And not criticisms.

But reflections: as someone who has stepped into this role at a time of real pressure across the financial system, and who has had the privilege of listening closely to consumers, members like you, and our own people at AFCA.

I don’t wish to be Captain Obvious here, but what’s struck me most since I joined is this: it is an exceptionally challenging time for both industry and Australian consumers. And it is an exceptionally demanding time to be working in dispute resolution.

The pressure is real. It has been building for some time. And it is being felt right across the system.

Consumers are under strain, often long before they ever pick up the phone to lodge a complaint. Claims teams are operating in an environment of growing complexity, scrutiny and expectation. And organisations like AFCA, sitting between consumers and industry, are managing higher volumes, more complexity, and greater vulnerability than we’ve seen before.

It’s no coincidence that tonight’s Federal Budget will be grappling with many of these same pressures at a national level: how we build resilience in an uncertain world, how we manage rising cost‑of‑living pressures, how we lift productivity and reform our tax system, and how we support people through periods of economic stress.

Those same themes play out every day in the claims system: in how quickly issues are resolved, how clearly expectations are set, and how effectively pressure is released before matters escalate.

So today, I don’t want to talk about what insurers should be doing. I think you are well placed to be thinking about your own organisations.

Instead, I want to share four things:

I think this Summit is a timely and important moment to have this conversation.

  • what we’re collectively experiencing,
  • what we’re seeing through complaints data and disputes,
  • what we’re exploring internally at AFCA,
  • and how together, we might release some of the pressure that’s building, so fewer issues escalate, trust is maintained, and outcomes improve for everyone involved.

The environment we’re operating in

To understand what’s happening in complaints and more specifically for claims right now, we have to start with the broader environment we’re operating in.

We are all working in a volatile and uncertain world.

War continues to disrupt supply chains. Inflation and cost‑of‑living pressures are putting additional strain on families. Energy prices, housing costs, and everyday expenses are forcing many people to make trade‑offs they haven’t had to consider before.

For a growing number of consumers, financial stress is new. What we are seeing and what the Financial Counsellors told me in Cairns last week is that people who would normally feel secure are suddenly vulnerable. And when that’s the backdrop, even small issues with an insurer, a bank, or a financial firm can feel overwhelming.

Delays feel longer. Uncertainty feels heavier. And when things don’t go smoothly, people escalate faster.

The result is increased pressure across the entire system. I hear this firsthand because I sit on the floor with our customer service teams and I overhear the calls with consumers each day. Even as they are ringing us, my teams tell me the calls are feeling more pressured and consumers don’t know who to call in that moment, so they often call us.

[SLIDE]

At AFCA, we’re seeing that play out clearly in the numbers. In the first quarter of this year alone, from January to March, we received more than 30,000 complaints across all products, representing a 23 per cent increase on the same period last year. On current trends, we expect to receive around 120,000 complaints this financial year, up from 100,000 last year.

Claims complaints across General Insurance, Life Insurance, and Superannuation are a significant part of that picture. Over the same January to March period, complaints that included a claim as an issue increased by 38 per cent. Almost a quarter of all complaints we received during that time involved a claim.

But I want to be very clear about something, in my view.

This is not a story about declining standards. It is not a story about firms disengaging from customers. And it is not a story about lack of effort or care.

It is a story about a system under sustained stress.

In a climate like this, it would be easy to focus only on what’s going wrong. But that wouldn’t be a full or fair picture of what’s happening in the claims sector.

One of the most encouraging things I’ve observed since stepping into this role is the level of effort, adaptability and professionalism across members – even under this sustained pressure.

Despite growing public scrutiny, increased regulatory attention, and unprecedented claim volumes, many insurers have been proactive in how they support customers.

And nowhere is that clearer than in the way significant events and natural disasters have been handled.

I’ll talk more about this shortly, but I want to say upfront: what we are seeing in many parts of the system is evidence of people working incredibly hard to do the right thing in very difficult conditions.

That matters. And it deserves recognition.

Releasing pressure by nailing the fundamentals

When pressure builds, things naturally get more complicated. That’s when the basics really matter – they’re often what stop issues from escalating.

Clear communication. Reliable timeframes. Transparency in decision‑making. Closing the loop.

These aren’t new concepts. But in periods of uncertainty, their importance multiplies.

Delays feel different when someone is already under financial or emotional strain. A lack of clarity can quickly become a lack of trust. And trust, once eroded, is hard to rebuild.

This challenge, of course, isn’t unique to industry.

At AFCA, we’re experiencing it too.

We’ve started the year with record complaint numbers, increasing complexity across matters, and a higher proportion of complainants experiencing vulnerability. And that’s forcing us to look closely at how we deliver our own service under pressure.

Our focus has been on how AFCA shows up as a stable, dependable, and reliable and empathetic part of the system, particularly when things feel uncertain elsewhere.

At AFCA, that comes down to a renewed focus on four fundamentals: timeliness, communication, transparency, and closing the loop. It’s what I refer to with my team as doing the basics.

Timeliness isn’t about rushing decisions (it's not about prolonging them either). It’s about setting realistic expectations and meeting them. It’s about what happens at each step of a complaint: consistent extensions, prompt responses when new information arises, and reducing friction as matters progress. We have some work to do on this.

And when timelines can’t be met, proactive communication becomes even more important. Clear explanations about unavoidable delays, given early, can reset expectations and keep people engaged in the process.

Transparency is especially critical at closure. Being open and clear about how a decision has been reached helps complainants better understand, and often accept, the outcome.

And finally, closing the loop matters. Even strong processes have blind spots. Taking the time to confirm that all issues have been addressed reassures people that they haven’t been forgotten.

There’s no silver bullet here. But my hope is that by focusing on these fundamentals we can significantly reduce the likelihood of complaints escalating and improve the experience for everyone involved. I acknowledge that we need to reduce the time it is currently taking to allocate our cases and reach a conclusion.

What the data tells us about claims pressure points

So, what does AFCA data tell us about claims complaints?

To start with, claims complaints are up almost 40 per cent at the beginning of this year, now making up around 24 per cent of all complaints we receive.

But here’s a critical point that often gets lost: around 45 per cent of these claim complaints are resolved at referral back to insurers, through their own IDR processes.

That tells us something important. Many claim issues are being resolved effectively without ever requiring AFCA to determine them. It would of course be better if they had never come to us, especially the less complex ones, but they do, and they are resolved through IDR.

Of the complaints that do return to us, more of those require decisions, reflecting the growing complexity of matters we’re seeing. Even so, resolutions this year have been, on average, 19 days faster than last year.

The most common issues remain familiar and will not come as any surprise to you: claim delays, claim denials, and disputes about claim amounts.

Claim delays in particular have been a long‑standing pressure point. This year, we’ve seen a 28 per cent increase in complaints raising delay as an issue. Given the environment we’re operating in, especially supply‑chain disruption, that pressure is likely to continue. We know from talking with the ICA, and with your teams, that this is already very real due to world events – things like access to plastic pipes and car parts.

Avoidable and unavoidable delays

But one distinction that’s becoming increasingly important is the difference between avoidable and unavoidable delays.

Avoidable delays are those we all want to see less of: missed follow‑ups, breakdowns in oversight, or failures to act when things don’t go to plan. They’re often the result of contractors not going forward with planned works when agreed or taking longer than planned on certain works.

We recognise that these complaints may not be caused by the direct actions of the claims manager or the insurer. However, you are responsible for overseeing your contractors. It’s important to maintain oversight of how settlement plans are progressing and if there are delays, the consumer needs to be informed of why, and of the new timeline.

These are the complaints that often escalate quickly and where non‑financial loss may need to be considered. Under AFCA’s Approach to non-financial loss, which is available on our website, we can consider this when there has been an unusual amount of time taken to resolve a situation – often the case following delays.

Unavoidable delays are different. Supply‑chain disruption is a real, system‑wide issue. Waiting for overseas parts. Specialist labour shortages. Global transport delays. These are generally not within an insurer’s control.

What we often see, though, is that it’s not the delay itself that drives the complaint. It’s the uncertainty around it. When customers don’t know why something is delayed, how long it’s likely to take, or what’s happening in the meantime, frustration builds, and complaints follow.

In many cases, the delay is unavoidable, but the escalation is not.

We don’t expect firms to be immune from the current environment. We recognise that many of the complaints reaching us are not the result of poor intent or poor practice. And the data supports that.

Regardless, we know consumers are feeling the squeeze, and extra vigilance to ensure claims are being responded to promptly and with open and clear communication will reduce complaints and escalations.

Significant events

Another area with growing pressure on insurers is in significant event claims, such as more recently, hailstorms – I vividly remember that weekend storm in Sydney.

I’m sure everyone in the room today has felt Australia’s dramatic increase in significant weather conditions. Last year, insurers handled close to 300,000 claims from extreme weather events, almost six times the number from the year before. That scale is unprecedented.

While we have seen substantial growth in the number of significant event complaints reaching AFCA, with complaint volumes doubling to start the year, the increase pales in comparison to the overall number of significant event claims being made.

That gap matters.

It tells us that although the system is under immense pressure, much of that pressure is being absorbed – through your preparation, customer‑focused processes, and extraordinary effort across claims teams.

It reflects an enormous amount of hard work across the sector to adapt quickly and support customers through incredibly challenging circumstances.

Similarly, as at the end of April, we had received just seven complaints relating to the Middle East conflict.

That speaks volumes about how quickly and effectively risks are being identified and managed.

These statistics aren’t about downplaying issues. They’re about understanding where the system is holding, what are you doing to achieve it, and what we can learn from that.

Complaints as early warning signs

One of the reasons I’ve spent time on the complaints data today is because it tells us more than just where things have gone wrong. It also tells us where pressure is building before it becomes systemic.

Complaints don’t arise in isolation. They often point to underlying pressure points in processes, communications or decision‑making. One complaint can reveal an issue that, left unaddressed, becomes many more.

AFCA’s data is one part of that picture. Tools like the AFCA Datacube provides monthly data updates that allow firms to benchmark and identify trends. Within the AFCA Member Portal, insurers can also access our Member Benchmarking Dashboard. We are keen to work with you to understand how we can leverage our data better.

But our data is most powerful when combined with your own internal insights: IDR outcomes, customer feedback, frontline observations, is just as important, if not more so.

In many cases, looking beyond formal complaints to repeated calls, emails or questions can reveal areas where customers are confused or expectations aren’t being set early enough.

You all know this, but a complaint can be a signal that something in your business is not working as it should. This can alert you to an underlying issue that could lead to bigger risks. Looking at complaints as early warning indicators can mean inherent risks are addressed early.

For instance, we recently worked with a firm where a series of claims delay complaints revealed not just timing issues, but misalignment between policy wording, procedures and communication. Addressing that alignment, through clearer processes, training and assurance, resolved the underlying issue before it escalated further.

We alerted the firm to these issues, and to their credit, they immediately tried to remedy them. Firstly, revising their procedures to ensure consistency with the policy. This can be a common issue for firms, where practice doesn’t reflect policy or written procedure. It also led to new training for claims staff to improve customer communication and committed to periodic assurance reviews of their claims handling quality.

AFCA’s data, case studies, and systemic insights are derived from the cases that reach us at the end of the complaints journey. Many of you already implement reviews and checks, like this, to ensure your practices remain effective and standards don’t slip, and those cases never escalate to us.

What good IDR looks like under pressure

At the heart of all of this is Internal Dispute Resolution.

Ultimately, effective IDR is one of the most powerful ways to prevent escalation. Particularly when delays or complexity are unavoidable.

A consumer making a claim is often already dealing with one of the worst days of their life, whether that’s a damaged home, a broken car, or being stranded far from home. When processes feel unclear or impersonal, trust erodes quickly.

Identifying vulnerability early is critical. We’ve seen more than a 30 per cent increase this year in claims complaints involving vulnerability or the need for additional assistance. Practical actions, like temporary accommodation, hire vehicles, quick access to medical support, or proactive check‑ins, make a real difference to outcomes and confidence.

Clear, plain‑English communication matters just as much. Customers don’t need to be claims experts. They need to understand what’s happening, why a decision has been made, and what happens next.

At AFCA, we are taking a similar approach under a project called Equity of Access, which I know have the privilege of sponsoring. This program is a multi‑year effort to remove barriers and make AFCA’s service more accessible but it is more than that it is a fundamental culture change. Internally, we describe this through a simple idea: See the person.

It means seeing the person behind the complaint. Listening carefully, communicating clearly, and focusing on resolving the issue, not just progressing a case file.

There is a strong parallel here for firms. Many complaints escalate not because they are inherently complex, but because the customer has felt unheard, confused, or unable to get a clear and timely response. We believe, the same principles that underpin our Equity of Access work are also some of the most effective ways to reduce escalation at IDR.

In claims, this means recognising how the issue causing the claim may be prompting vulnerability. A consumer may be trapped in a foreign country, unable to stay in their own home, or lose hours daily in transit after a car breakdown.

I’m encouraged by the great work we see in this sector to offer temporary accommodation or rented vehicles. These steps, which identify and address vulnerabilities, are crucial to maintaining a positive relationship and trust with your customers.

Again, consistent and clear communication cannot be underestimated.

Crucial to this is using common, plain-English language and straightforward explanations. Remembering that you need to meet customers at their level.

A customer won’t have experience in claims handling or might not understand the specifics of a settlement. When firms explain their decisions and how they got there, and importantly, show proper consideration of the complainant’s arguments, they are more likely to accept an outcome.

Good IDR also means avoiding a “set and forget” approach. Building in reminders, oversight and physical assessments where appropriate to ensure issues don’t drift.

Working with AFCA

My vision as the inaugural Chief Customer Officer is that AFCA is not just the end of the line in disputes. My vision is that we, alongside you, play an important part in ensuring there continues to be trust and confidence in our financial systems – this is so important right now.

That is why many of our resources are designed to support firms earlier and to prevent complaints from arising: through published determinations, data insights, e‑learning modules, and guidance on common complaint issues such as claim delays.

While it would be great not to have complaints and to do myself out of a job, that is not the reality, so when complaints do reach us, we want the process to be as clear and workable as possible.

We publish our determinations on our website. While every determination is based on the unique circumstances of the complaint, these offer important insights into our considerations and decision-making processes.

Reviewing determinations on topics relevant to you can help to identify where and how complaints arise and strengthen your own systems in those areas.

The Datacube is a great asset to combine with your own internal complaints data to identify trends and unearth systemic issues. We continue to refine and improve it.

We also recently launched our first e-learning module, breaking down proximate cause and sharing insight on how we consider this key insurance concept during complaints. I’d encourage you to check it out.

These tools can help you during IDR, or to avoid complaints altogether.

If a matter is not resolved in IDR and the complaint does reach AFCA, we want to make sure our service is accessible and easy for you.

We have many supporting resources on our website, helping you to understand our processes and what is expected of you (as well as the consumer) during a complaint.

We have EDR guides for different complaint issues – including a recently updated guide for claim delays – setting out the information you should provide to AFCA at the start of a complaint. This allows us to quickly assess the merit of the complaint and determine the key issues we may need to consider.

We regularly publish new AFCA Approach documents to help consumers and financial firms better understand how we reach decisions about key issues. These documents explain the way we approach some common issues and complaint types that we see at AFCA.

Outside of these online resources, we also want to provide a service that works for you. If you have a complaint with us and need an extension on a deadline, let us know. If the extension is a necessity, we’ll work with you and the complainant to agree on new deadlines. If you don’t tell us when you need more time, we don’t know.

I really want us to appreciate that the customer experience is the entire journey, not just a single interaction at the time of complaint, and that the customer journey starts when the policy is purchased. At our best, AFCA, consumers and firms are working toward the same outcome, even when we play different roles. That’s something I really believe in.

Closing

There’s no question this is a challenging period for the claims sector. Pressure and uncertainty are real, and they’re unfortunately likely to remain part of the landscape for some time.

But what I’m encouraged by is the evidence that a focus on fundamentals, early insight and collaboration is already making a difference.

At AFCA, we want to use our position to help relieve pressure in the system: supporting consumers, supporting firms, and ultimately reducing the number of matters that ever need formal dispute resolution.

The work being done across this sector matters. It matters to individual customers on difficult days, and it matters to trust in the financial system more broadly.

By staying connected, sharing insight, and learning from emerging pressures together, we can strengthen outcomes, not just for our own organisations, but for the system as a whole. Together, let’s do the basics brilliantly.

Thank you.

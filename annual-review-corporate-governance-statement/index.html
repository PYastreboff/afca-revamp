Introduction

Our approach to governance

With our vision ‘towards an Australia free from financial disputes’, the Australian Financial Complaints Authority Limited (AFCA) is committed ‘to provide fair, independent and effective solutions for financial disputes.’

At AFCA, corporate governance is the framework of the relationships, systems, policies and processes within which we operate, by which our authority is exercised, and through which we are held accountable. We recognise that our governance framework affects all our stakeholders, including our members, customers, employees and the community. It establishes the roles and responsibilities across our entire value chain, including the AFCA Board and its committees, AFCA management, our members, consumers, our employees and our suppliers.

We are committed to maintaining high standards of corporate governance and ensuring that our governance practices promote accountability, transparency and integrity in all our operations.

Operation of the AFCA Board and its governance framework

The AFCA Board

The AFCA Board is an independent group of directors with extensive experience in consumer affairs, industry, management and governance. It ensures that AFCA fulfils its duties as mandated by its constitution and the Corporations Act, while providing an independent, fair, accountable, efficient, and effective service supported by strong corporate governance.

The Board provides leadership and strategic guidance, overseeing the sound and prudent management of the organisation and the implementation of AFCA’s strategic objectives. It strives for excellence in governance standards and practices, maintaining the proper culture of the organisation, and the independence of the AFCA Scheme and its decision‑making process.

Since its inception, AFCA’s Board has adopted a Charter that governs its operations.

The Charter outlines the role, responsibilities and composition of the Board and the way it discharges its responsibilities. It also sets out the respective roles and responsibilities of the Board and management, those matters reserved expressly for the Board and those that may be delegated to management. It also outlines the roles and responsibilities of key individuals including ombudsmen, adjudicators, panel members, the Company Secretary, and the Independent Assessor. It is reviewed annually.

Please note that AFCA’s Board does not involve itself in the detail of individual complaints lodged with AFCA.

The Board’s Charter is available on our website.

Audit and Risk Committee

Responsible for overseeing financial reporting, risk management, and internal controls. The Audit and Risk Committee regularly reviews the effectiveness of AFCA’s risk management framework and internal controls to ensure they remain robust and effective.

Nominations Committee

Responsible for Board appointments, governance policies, and Board evaluations.

In line with AFCA’s practices, the Nominations Committee ensures that the Board composition reflects a balance of skills, experience and diversity, and oversees the succession planning of the Board and its Committees. The Committee is Chaired by the Independent Chair of the Board and must include the Chair of the Audit and Risk Committee, and the Chair of the People and Remuneration Committee.

People and Remuneration Committee

Responsible for setting remuneration policies and practices.

The People and Remuneration Committee ensures that AFCA’s remuneration policies are aligned with our strategic objectives and promote long‑term value creation.

This Committee also has both oversight and review of AFCA’s organisation culture, inclusion and diversity, and health, safety and wellbeing. It oversees the succession, talent planning and performance of AFCA’s senior leadership and management team.

Delegation of authority

Employees are delegated authority by the Board to make designated business decisions about the operations of AFCA. Certain employees are provided with authorisation to act on behalf of AFCA in exercising statutory powers, such as delegations concerning AFCA’s superannuation statutory powers.

In accordance with good governance practice, AFCA keeps a formal written list of delegation of authority that details which of the Board’s authority under AFCA’s Constitution are delegated to the Chief Ombudsman, and the Chief Ombudsman’s sub‑delegations to various AFCA employees. Matters reserved for the Board are clearly stated in the Delegations Matrix and the AFCA Board’s Charter.

Director appointment process

The Board, with the assistance of the Nominations Committee, conducts a formal selection process before appointing new Non Executive Directors to AFCA’s Board and its Committees.

Professional consultants are engaged as required to identify prospective Director candidates.

Upon a recommendation from the Nominations Committee, the Board evaluates Director candidates against AFCA’s Board Guidelines for Director Appointment and Re‑Appointment. This outlines the strict criteria for both Directors with industry experience and those with consumer experience, and the selection process or re‑appointment process. It also outlines the due diligence requirements for appointment to the AFCA Board, including criminal record and bankruptcy checks, and checks of the person’s educational qualifications and employment history.

Tenure and composition

Non‑Executive Directors of AFCA’s Board are not required to stand for re‑election by rotation at the Annual General Meeting each year.

However, a Director holds office for an initial term of up to three years from the date of their appointment by AFCA’s Board. It is the Board’s policy that a Director is eligible for re‑appointment upon conclusion of that term of up to a maximum of two terms (or six years) (unless the Directors determine it would be appropriate for the Director to serve one further, final term of a maximum of three years).

The Directors on AFCA’s Board represent a range of ages and backgrounds. The Board composition also includes longer‑serving Directors who have a deeper knowledge of AFCA together with newer Directors.

Director induction and education

All new Directors of AFCA participate in an induction program to assist them in understanding AFCA, its operations and the AFCA Scheme, as well as AFCA’s governance, risk management, technology strategy and cyber security, environmental and social issues, audit, and regulatory and compliance matters. The induction process mirrors that provided to AFCA’s employees to ensure that Directors fully understand the culture and values of the organisation and are given a ‘client voice’ or consumer perspective of the AFCA Scheme and complaints process.

A continuing education program is incorporated into the Board’s calendar, which enables Directors, individually and collectively, to develop and maintain the skills and knowledge needed to support the Board’s decision‑making.

The Board also attended several targeted education sessions during 2024‑25, including externally facilitated sessions allowing Directors to gain a deeper level of knowledge and insight into cyber security, AI and the mandatory climate‑related financial disclosure requirements under the Australian Accounting Standards Board’s (AASB’s) S2 Climate‑related Disclosures.

Remuneration

The People and Remuneration Committee is the governing body for developing, monitoring and assessing the remuneration strategy and framework across AFCA on behalf of the Board, ensuring that these are aligned to regulatory requirements, appropriate and operating as intended. The role of the People and Remuneration Committee is to review, challenge, assess and as appropriate, endorse the recommendations made by management for Board approval. The Board reviews, challenges, applies judgement and as appropriate, approves the People and Remuneration Committee’s recommendations.

All Directors are Non‑Executive Directors, and apart from the Chair, are paid equally. The Chairs of the People and Remuneration Committee and Audit and Risk Committee are paid an additional small fee in recognition of their extra commitment and workload.

During 2024‑25, the Board, assisted by both the Nominations Committee and the People and Remuneration Committee, has undertaken an extensive external review of the Board and Committee Remuneration Framework, benchmarking the framework against similar not‑for‑profit entities within the financial services sector, to ensure that the framework is effective and remains aligned with the needs of the organisation and its stakeholders, including AFCA’s own employees. The framework is annually reviewed internally by the Board to ensure its continued effectiveness, with an external review being undertaken at least once every three years.

Another of the Board and its Committee’s responsibilities is to establish and assess executive performance and remuneration targets and outcomes, including that of the Chief Ombudsman and Chief Executive Office.

When determining annual performance scorecards, the Board considers past performance, internal forecasts and external expectations, to set targets that are stretching and aligned to strategic areas of focus. This is done on an annual basis with assistance from both the Nominations Committee and the People and Remuneration Committee.

Board evaluation

The AFCA Board strives to continuously monitor and improve its performance, as well as that of its Committees and individual Directors.

Under its Charter, AFCA’s Board must annually assess its performance and the performance of individual Directors. Additionally, an external facilitated performance and capability review of the Board and its Committees is required at least once every three years.

In November 2024, an independent external evaluation assessed the performance of the Board, each Committee and individual Directors. The process included the completion of a survey by the entire Board and individual interviews with Directors. It also evaluated the effectiveness of the Board and its Committee processes, the appropriateness of matters discussed at meetings and the key areas of focus for the Board. The findings were presented to the Board and considered at a workshop for the Board in December 2024.

The Board, with assistance from the Nominations Committee, evaluates the performance of Directors retiring and seeking re‑appointment to the Board. The Board uses the results of those evaluations when considering the endorsement of Directors for re‑appointment.

Ethical responsibility

Conflicts management

AFCA Directors have a continuing responsibility to determine whether they have a potential or actual conflict of interest, in relation to any material matter which relates to the affairs of AFCA. Such a situation may arise from external associations, interests or personal relationships.

This is particularly pertinent given AFCA Directors will have either industry experience, or will have experience within consumer advocacy, however, they must be sufficiently independent so as to bring an impartial view to decisions referred before the Board, ensuring those decisions are made in the best interests of AFCA and all its relevant stakeholders.

AFCA has both an organisation wide Conflict Management Policy and Procedure and a specific Director Conflict Management Policy and Procedure. The latter emphasises that Directors do not perform a representative function on behalf of consumers, consumer groups or the industry, but instead are appointed as Directors to contribute their experience to the Board, with independence.

Given the uniqueness of the background requirements of Directors arising under the Constitution, Directors should be cognisant of conflicts and ensure any actual or perceived differences are managed appropriately and in accordance with this policy and the broader Corporations Act 2001 (Cth).

In support of this policy, Directors are required to review the Board’s Register of Conflicts and Interests at every Board and/or Committee meeting and to declare any interests relevant to the matters to be discussed at those meetings. Furthermore, an Annual Declaration of Interests is required to be made by all AFCA Directors to ensure there is sufficient information to manage conflicts appropriately, as required.

In addition to the above, all Directors are asked to notify the Independent Chair before accepting any new outside appointments. The Chair reviews the proposed new appointment and considers any issue on an individual basis and where applicable, also the issue of more than one Director serving on the same outside board or other body, as well as the potential fettering of the Director’s ability to exert independent judgement, particularly where such an appointment would be in connection with a Member of AFCA or significant consumer representative body.

Code of conduct

AFCA has an organisational wide Code of Conduct (‘the Code’) that applies to both employees and Non‑Executive Directors. It supports AFCA’s values and provides both employees and the Board with a practical set of guiding principles to help them make fair, balanced and ethical decisions in their day‑to‑day work and decision‑making at Board level. The Code also recognises the different responsibilities that Directors have under the law and promotes those, together with the broader values and principles provided under the Code. The Codes require honesty, integrity, quality and trust.

The Code is reviewed regularly (at least every two years) and any changes are presented to the People and Remuneration Committee for review and input and endorsement (where appropriate) for approval by the Board.

Whistleblowing

AFCA is committed to fostering a culture where our people and others feel safe to speak up about matters or conduct that concerns them.

Whistleblowers perform an essential function in the community, ensuring that public officials are held to account and private individuals operate within the confines of the law. Protection of whistleblowers, is therefore, integral to fostering transparency, promoting integrity and detecting misconduct.

Fraud, corruption and anti‑bribery

AFCA is committed to maintaining the highest standards of integrity and ethical conduct within its own operations.

Effective fraud and corruption control is an integral part of good management practice and governance. AFCA is committed to having an organisational culture, which ensures that effective fraud risk management is embedded in AFCA’s business processes.

AFCA’s Fraud and Corruption Control Policy applies to both the Directors and employees of AFCA and in particular, outlines specific responsibilities for both the Board and the Audit and Risk Committee in ensuring systems, processes and capabilities are in place that supports AFCA’s zero tolerance approach to fraudulent or corrupt activity, and enables AFCA to respond effectively when fraud and corruption is suspected or has been identified.

Risk management

Board’s role in risk management

Risk management within AFCA is overseen by the Board and its supporting committees.

The AFCA Board is ultimately responsible for risk management at AFCA, and establishes our risk culture. All matters to be decided, discussed, and/or noted by the Board are mapped against AFCA’s material risks and strategic objectives.

At AFCA, we believe that our risk culture has an impact on the effectiveness of our risk management framework. We are committed to establishing a risk culture that proactively identifies, treats, and mitigates risks.

Risk management framework

AFCA’s risk management framework aligns to its best practice and Australian standards (ISO 31000:2018). AFCA has implemented a Risk Management Framework that provides a systematic and integrated approach to the proactive management of risks across our activities. AFCA’s risk management framework is underpinned with a strong risk culture and mandatory risk training.

The Risk Management Framework, supported by AFCA Risk Management Policy and Risk Appetite Statements, embeds risk management in AFCA’s business processes and ensures that risks are managed effectively and efficiently to deliver our stated outcomes and objectives.

Risk appetite statement

Risk appetite statements established by AFCA for its material risks are supported by quantifiable metrics. There is regular oversight and reporting of any metric outside agreed tolerance levels.

Risk register, controls and mitigations

In accordance with our Risk Management Framework, we conduct regular risk workshops and reviews to ensure our risk register, controls and mitigations, consider and effectively respond to changes to both the internal and external environment, and remain current.

Audit and Risk Committee

While ultimate responsibility for risk oversight and risk management rests with the full Board, the Audit and Risk Committee has oversight of these activities, and the Senior Leadership Group has day‑to‑day operational responsibility for risk oversight and management.

Risk reporting

To enable proper oversight of the organisation’s risk management, AFCA’s Board and Audit and Risk Committee receive regular quarterly reporting, and ensure that risk workshops are regularly conducted to consider AFCA’s risk profile and operating environment.

Scenario planning

As stewards of AFCA’s sustainability, our Board recognises the importance of planning for unexpected events, including cyber incidents. We schedule time regularly for scenario planning and document incident response strategies.

Stakeholder engagement

At AFCA, we recognise the importance of considering the interests of all our stakeholders while having regard for our purpose and strategic objectives. The AFCA Board actively engages with a wide range of AFCA stakeholders during 2024‑25 to ensure it is up‑to‑date with a diverse range of viewpoints and interests.

Consumers

The AFCA Board has an ongoing commitment to ensure that the consumer/complainant experience is considered in decision‑making, and that consumer needs are being met through its Strategic and Corporate Plan. The Board takes a ‘client voice’ perspective as part of its decision‑making.

Board activities

  • As part of AFCA’s Board induction process, each new Non‑Executive Director is provided an opportunity to sit with customer service teams to receive direct feedback from consumers.
  • The Board regularly meets with consumer representatives and advocacy groups.
  • The Board is actively engaged in the implementation and success of AFCA’s Equity of Access strategic pillar, which ensures fairness and equity across all of our services, so that every Australian can access the support they need.

Employees

The Board actively monitors AFCA’s culture, ensuring the organisational culture aligns with AFCA’s vision, purpose and values and that our people are supported and appropriately skilled.

Board activities

The Board regularly addresses a range of people and culture related topics and issues with assistance from both the Nominations Committee and the People and Remuneration Committee. Key activities included:

  • regularly reviewing and input into the Code of Conduct
  • oversight and input into the diversity, inclusion and the belonging strategy for AFCA, including the Gender Equity Action Program
  • considering and discussion of reports related to health, safety and wellbeing, including incident trends, significant investigations and assurance activity
  • regularly reviewing AFCA’s remuneration strategies and outcomes as well as succession planning and talent development.

Members

To enhance our service, industry practices and to reduce disputes, the Board collaborates closely with our members, engaging with both their complaint handling units and senior leadership, including their boards and executives.

Board activities

To allow as many members as possible the opportunity to attend meetings of members, AFCA makes these available online using webcast technology.

  • AFCA’s website.
  • Before the Annual General Meeting (AGM), members have the opportunity to submit questions to the Chair and/or Chief Ombudsman and CEO to enable those to be considered during the meeting. The External Auditor also attends AFCA’s AGMs and is available to answer member questions on any matter that concerns them in their capacity as auditor.
  • AFCA also aims to hold two Member Forums per year. These events include dedicated sessions on banking and finance, small business, superannuation, life insurance, general insurance, investments and advice and provide an opportunity for members to raise issues of concern. The Board is kept appraised of those Member Forums and the key themes that arise.

Government and regulators

The Board has the task of meaningful engagement with both Government and regulators to continue to build constructive relationships and to help secure AFCA’s vision of an Australia without financial services related disputes.

Board activities

  • The Board regularly invites Government stakeholders and regulators to its Board meetings throughout the year to discuss key industry issues, including regular engagements with ASIC, APRA and the ACCC.
  • The Board also seeks to meet with the Minister of Financial Services (and/or the Minister’s office) on a regular basis throughout the year.
  • AFCA is regularly represented by its Executive team at parliamentary committee meetings, including the House of Representatives Standing Committee on Economics.
  • AFCA is also regularly invited to provide submissions on parliamentary inquiries or consultations.

Interest groups

The Board recognises AFCA’s diverse range of stakeholders and the particular importance of consumer groups, which are working to assist AFCA in improving financial services, sharing insights, and resolving disputes fairly and independently.

Board activities

  • The Board regularly meets with a number of national consumer advisory committees and groups, including the Australian Consumer Advisory Panel (ACAP), to gain insights and analysis on both strategic and policy issues facing consumers.

Community

The Board recognises that to deliver long‑term sustainable outcomes for all AFCA stakeholders and to be a service accessible to all, it must understand the expectations of, and impact on, all relevant consumers and communities in Australia.

Board activities

  • The Board regularly receives reports on received the awareness of AFCA’s brand, and the level of trust and reputation which it has within the community.
  • The Board is in the process of approving AFCA’s 2026‑28 Innovate RAP to further its awareness and understanding of issues affecting indigenous Australian communities and consumers.
  • The Board is actively involved in implementing AFCA’s strategic pillar ‘Equity of Access’, which ensures AFCA and the AFCA Scheme remain relevant and available to all consumers, particularly to vulnerable consumers.

Supplier/Vendors

The AFCA Board understands the importance of fostering key supplier partnerships to help deliver its strategy.

Board activities

  • The Board regularly reviews the significant contractual relationships and expenditure for AFCA, to ensure value for money for its stakeholders and the effectiveness of its contractual engagements.

Sustainability

Sustainability and purpose

The Board is responsible for considering the environmental and social impact of AFCA’s activities, overseeing the implementation and effectiveness of AFCA’s sustainability strategy and approving AFCA’s upcoming climate‑related financial disclosures.

Key activities include:

  • Considering regular updates from Executive Management on the implementation and progress of AFCA’s sustainability strategy and progress towards compliance under AASB S1 and S2.
  • Approving the annual Modern Slavery Statement, which includes assessing the effectiveness of actions undertaken and commitments to be made for forthcoming years.
  • Considering updates on AFCA’s social impact, including its RAP.

Key complaint and industry trends

Financial difficulty persists despite decline in complaints

We received 4,764 financial difficulty complaints in 2024‑25 – 17% less than 2023‑24. While this suggests improved practices by financial firms, the volume of these complaints is still too high.

Notably, while overall financial difficulty complaints declined, complaints accepted at our Case Management stage trended upwards. This highlights that financial difficulty complaints could benefit from earlier attention and action from financial firms.

Financial firms still falling short in responding to requests for assistance

One of the top five most common banking and finance complaints, and number one in financial difficulty complaints, is the category of financial firms failing to respond to requests for assistance. There were 2,890 ‘failure to respond to request for assistance’ complaints in 2024‑25, compared to 2,838 complaints received in 2023‑24, despite an overall decline in financial difficulty complaints. This persistent gap in support is particularly concerning for consumers experiencing vulnerability, who should expect timely and compassionate engagement.

Only 42% of financial difficulty complaints were resolved at our first ‘Referral’ stage – significantly lower than for banking and finance complaints overall (59%). This suggests financial firms may not fully understand the specific needs of these customers, making it harder to assist them.

AFCA encourages financial firms to continually upskill around financial difficulty and extra care obligations, especially considering strengthened consumer protections in the 2025 Banking Code of Practice.

Opportunities for improvement in vulnerability and accessibility

Complaints frequently involved consumers in circumstances that placed them at increased risk of vulnerability, including those affected by mental illness, family breakdown or violence, or job loss. 5% of people in this group were First Nations peoples, while 1% used interpreting services. This highlights the importance of culturally safe and accessible financial assistance processes.

The updated Banking Code of Practice requires subscribing financial firms to:

Financial firms should continue to improve their assistance practices and build trust with diverse communities. Customer support can be enhanced through:

  • provide interpreter services free of charge
  • offer cultural awareness training to staff
  • simplify processes for appointing third‑party representatives
  • avoid selling debts when customers experience ongoing vulnerability.
  • early identification of financial difficulty and extra care needs, by being attuned to indicators of financial stress and accessibility needs
  • positive framing of assistance by emphasising benefits like paused enforcement action and avoiding adverse credit reporting to encourage engagement in hardship assistance options
  • reducing barriers to access by avoiding rigid interpreter policies and excessive verification requirements that hinder support.

Systemic issues and regulatory engagement

AFCA conducted systemic investigations through 2024‑25 into financial firms’ compliance with financial difficulty and extra care obligations. Concerns included:

Our investigations were informed by multiple complaints and AFCA determinations, revealing patterns of poor support for customers disclosing mental health concerns, family violence and other vulnerabilities. AFCA’s process is required to report relevant systemic issues to regulators, including ASIC and APRA.

  • failure to issue written notices when financial difficulty assistance is declined
  • inadequate processes for ‘extra care’ of customers experiencing vulnerability
  • policies and processes not fit to identify, and respond to requests for help.

Looking ahead

AFCA’s new Case Management System has refined the way issues are classified. We are considering even more granular enhancements to our financial difficulty issues classifications so we can enable more targeted insights and improved complaint handling.

As we continue to monitor trends and engage with financial firms, the message is clear: financial difficulty is a real and pressing issue. Meaningful, sustained improvements in financial difficulty support are essential and will benefit both consumers and financial firms. With strengthened obligations and clearer expectations, financial firms must use the tools available to better support Australians who are unable to meet their payment obligations.

Case study

Inappropriate hardship responses and debt collection conduct

Background

In May 2018, the complainant obtained a home loan from a bank to purchase a property. Shortly after, severe mental health issues, multiple hospitalisations and other health‑related challenges forced him to stop working.

Over several years, the complainant tried to establish a suitable financial hardship arrangement so he could keep his home. He reported persistent barriers and delays from the bank, which made accessing support difficult and subsequently sought legal assistance. The complainant described the bank’s conduct as lacking empathy and understanding, particularly since he was experiencing vulnerable circumstances.

Complaint

The complainant said the bank did not respond appropriately to his hardship notices; they issued premature decline letters and demanded too much documentation. He said they did not genuinely consider his situation, despite being told repeatedly about his mental health challenges. Even though he had an authorised representative to communicate with the bank on his behalf, the bank continued to contact him directly, against the Debt Collection Guideline for Collectors and Creditors (April 2021). They also kept asking for letters of authority when they already had one. This created further stress and unnecessary obstacles.

The complainant said the bank incorrectly reported adverse repayment history information on his credit file, which added to his distress.

Outcome

AFCA found that while the bank occasionally met its financial hardship obligations, it was inconsistent. The bank was found to have:

AFCA determined the bank’s conduct did not align with the Banking Code of Practice, particularly its commitment to supporting customers experiencing vulnerability. Their debt collection practices also breached Clause 9 of the Debt Collection Guideline, which requires respectful and lawful engagement with both the customer and their representative.

Although the complainant demonstrated no financial loss, AFCA awarded $23,900 compensation for non‑financial loss, due to stress and inconvenience caused by the bank’s actions. This cumulative amount was reached owing to multiple separate failures being made out within the one complaint. AFCA’s determination provided the complainant with fair compensation, and required the financial firm to meet its hardship obligations.

  • not responded to hardship notices adequately, or in required timeframes
  • issued premature decline notices
  • requested unnecessary documentation
  • failed to provide meaningful support to a customer experiencing vulnerability.

Case study

Adequate support for a customer experiencing vulnerability

Background

In 2016, a financial firm approved a joint car loan for the complainant and her then partner. The loan had a 60‑month term with a final balloon payment of $28,945 due in June 2021. The complainant, a victim‑survivor of family violence – including coercive control and financial abuse – later fled the relationship with her young daughter and kept the car.

No payments were made after February 2021. When contacted by the financial firm in March 2021 about the overdue loan, the complainant disclosed her experience of family violence, her lack of awareness of the loan, and her desire to keep the vehicle, despite being unable to make repayments.

Complaint

The complainant told AFCA that her ex‑partner had controlled all financial matters and likely scanned her signature from another document to get the loan, without her knowledge or consent. She was unaware of the terms and raised concerns about its legitimacy and the effect on her financial wellbeing. She expressed concern about adverse credit listings and the potential long‑term impact on her financial autonomy.

Outcome

AFCA accepted the complainant’s account, in line with the Family Violence Approach. There was no indication the financial firm was aware of her circumstances before March 2021, and AFCA found they had acted appropriately once notified.

Over two years, the financial firm provided tailored hardship assistance, allowing time for the complainant to pursue family court matters and explore options like refinancing. They communicated clearly, offered practical solutions, and ultimately suggested she give up the vehicle. The financial firm said it would not pursue any shortfall.

Although the complainant suffered no financial loss, AFCA agreed that the financial firm should not pursue any shortfall, pending family court orders, as the loan was obtained without her informed consent.

AFCA found the financial firm had met its financial hardship and vulnerability obligations. Given the complainant’s circumstances however, AFCA thought it unfair for adverse credit information to remain on her file; therefore, the financial firm was required to remove it.

Case study

Failure to genuinely consider a customer’s needs and placing barriers to accessing support

Background

In July 2015, the complainant obtained a bank’s home loan secured by a mortgage. Over time, she experienced significant financial hardship due to serious mental and physical health issues, which affected her ability to work and meet loan repayments.

The complainant contacted the bank multiple times to seek assistance. She disclosed her deteriorating mental health, unemployment and need for a representative to help communicate with the bank. Despite her disclosures, the bank provided no adequate support or genuine consideration for her situation.

Complaint

The complainant lodged a complaint with AFCA, saying the bank:

  • made it difficult for her to access assistance for financial hardship
  • failed to provide extra care, despite awareness of her health issues
  • did not accept her representative’s appointment in a timely or reasonable way
  • offered short‑term repayments without properly assessing her capacity to pay
  • mishandled her financial hardship requests in ways that caused undue stress and worsened her health.

Outcome

AFCA found the bank did not meet its obligations under the National Credit Code and Banking Code of Practice. It failed to:

AFCA determined the bank’s conduct caused significant stress and inconvenience. Although the complainant had since recovered and resumed repayments, the bank’s earlier failures warranted compensation. It was required to pay $6,700 for non‑financial loss directly to the complainant. The loan contract was not varied, as the complainant was able to meet her obligations.

This case highlights the importance of financial firms genuinely considering a customer’s unique circumstances and needs, providing support and extra care, and not putting up unnecessary barriers to assistance.

  • genuinely consider the complainant’s circumstances
  • provide tailored support, or request relevant financial information
  • accept appointment of the complainant’s representative in a timely and appropriate way
  • respond appropriately to her hardship notices.

Case study

Small business financial difficulty assistance

Background

In August 2018, the complainant and their spouse obtained a joint commercial loan from a financial firm. The loan was secured by a mortgage over a commercial property. The complainant’s business was significantly impacted by the COVID‑19 pandemic, which led to financial difficulty and an inability to meet loan repayments.

Over several years, the complainant sought financial difficulty assistance from the financial firm who provided multiple repayment moratoriums and arrangements, but the complainant was ultimately unable to sell the secured property, or bring the loan back to a serviceable position. In March 2025, the loan reached over $500,000 in arrears.

Complaint

The complainant raised concerns that the financial firm:

The complainant requested more time to sell the property and avoid enforcement action. They argued that the financial firm’s expectations were unreasonable given their financial position and ongoing efforts to sell the property.

  • stopped assistance, despite ongoing financial difficulty
  • required unaffordable increased repayments while attempting to sell the secured property
  • did not provide enough time or flexibility to resolve the situation through a property sale.

Outcome

AFCA found the financial firm had generally responded well to the complainant’s requests for assistance. It provided moratoriums and considered requests for assistance over a three‑year period. One decision by the financial firm – requiring the complainant to significantly increase repayments while attempting to sell the property – was not appropriate.

Despite this, AFCA concluded the financial firm had acted fairly overall and was entitled to decline further assistance. The complainant had not demonstrated an ability to repay the loan, even under varied terms, and it was in substantial arrears.

AFCA did not vary the loan contract. The financial firm had to provide a final three‑month repayment moratorium and the complainant had to sell. They could either provide a signed agency agreement to sell the property, or hand it over for the financial firm to sell. If a sale contract was not provided within the moratorium period, the financial firm would be entitled to resume enforcement action.

This outcome balanced fairness with finality, recognising both the complainant’s efforts, and allowing the financial firm to recover the debt.

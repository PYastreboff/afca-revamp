Key complaint trends

Banking and finance complaints were 9% lower for 2024‑25 when compared to 2023‑24. Most notable was a 57% drop in unauthorised transactions and 46% drop in scam‑related complaints. Service quality complaints increased by 5% and incorrect fees or costs charged rose by 22%.

While overall complaints to AFCA have decreased when compared to the record highs over the past two years, banking and finance complaints continue to be the largest complaint type, making up 54% of all complaints. This is still simply too many complaints, and AFCA seeks continued focus from banking and finance industry members to resolve more complaints earlier in the process.

  • Financial difficulty complaints
  • Expanding Jurisdiction: Building on trust and capability.

Resolution and timeframes

Although banking and finance complaints were down 9% compared to 2023‑24, closures were down by only 4%. Compensation awarded in closed complaints rose by 10%.

The time it took to resolve complaints remained stable, but we saw:

  • A 7% increase in cases closed within 31‑61 days (32% of all closures).
  • A significant 147% increase in cases taking over 365 days to close, which accounted for 2% of closures. This small but significant group of aged complaints arose from two years of unprecedentedly high complaints in the area, including complex and batched complaints. Significant work has been undertaken to efficiently process these complaints, including utilising a streamlined ‘lead decision’ process, adding additional resources and workshop complex aged complaints.
  • 59% of banking and finance complaints were resolved at the Referral stage, which showed that financial firms have remained committed to resolving issues early.

Industry trends and challenges

Overall volumes

Banking and finance complaints include unauthorised transactions, responsible lending, credit reporting, debt management and debt repair, financial difficulty requests for assistance and a broad range of other matters including service‑related complaints. Banking and finance services are increasingly digital in nature, and complex products and services often involve less or no human interaction. Although complaints have fallen by 9% after two years of record highs, banking and finance remains the largest category of incoming complaints. This shows the ongoing challenges consumers face in receiving fair treatment and clear information in day‑to‑day banking and finance engagements.

Especially given challenging economic conditions continue for many people, AFCA is calling on financial firms to pay closer attention to consumers experiencing vulnerability and those who need clearer communication and better support to access products and services. Resolving complaints lodged with financial firms earlier, before they escalate to EDR, can save a significant amount of time and money while building greater trust and confidence.

We continued to see responsible lending issues affecting people experiencing vulnerability. Common issues included:

Some consumers are more at risk of credit‑related harm because of family violence, coercion or control and other forms of financial abuse, existing financial difficulties, or other personal circumstances that make navigating financial services challenging. As lending assessments become more automated, particularly for products where margins are lower and more efficient processes are employed, it is vital that robust controls are retained to detect and investigate warning signs of financial difficulties, vulnerability and financial abuse. Sometimes the lender only dealt with one of the joint borrowers. A borrower who is financially abusive or controlling can hide the warning signs that the co‑borrow is being coerced into substantial loans without their free and informed consent.

Irresponsible lending can see people lose family homes or essential vehicles, and it might take years to financially and emotionally recover. This can drive them toward other forms of short‑term debt, making the situation worse.

Financial difficulty

Failure to respond to a request for financial difficulty assistance remains in the top five most common banking and finance complaints. This highlights the need for financial firms to provide more proactive, clearly communicated and empathetic support. Case study

Compassionate outcome negotiated with Ombudsman

Background

The complainant and her late husband refinanced a home loan. The husband was diagnosed with a terminal illness and received a lump sum from his superannuation fund and life insurance which he used to repay most of the home loan.

During the period of his illness, the husband redrew approximately $550,000 against the home loan without the complainant knowing of it.

When her late husband passed away a few years later, the complainant discovered this significant debt which she thought had been repaid fully using life insurance and superannuation lump sum payments.

As the complainant’s late husband was the sole family earner, she had limited capacity to make repayments.

Complaint

According to the complainant, the 2016 loan was unsuitable because:

The complainant also said the bank should have contacted her about the unusual and large redraws her late husband had made after the loan had almost been fully repaid.

  • her late husband controlled the family’s finances
  • the family was already in financial difficulty
  • the redraw facility created additional risk for her
  • the bank never spoke to her directly during the application process.

Outcome

The bank acknowledged the complainant’s unusual and unfortunate circumstances, and that she was in danger of losing her home.

The complainant accepted the outcome, and the complaint was resolved by agreement. This was a positive negotiated outcome for all parties and highlights the benefits of seeking resolution by agreement even in protracted complaints.

  • reduce the outstanding loan by over $180,000
  • reduce the interest rate

  • extend the loan term to allow the complainant to fully repay the loan at the lower repayment amount.

Case study

Independent legal advice

On 9 June 2005, the complainant, with the assistance of a broker, obtained an equity release product (the loan) from the financial firm. The complainant passed away on 27 July 2022 and the complaint was brought on behalf of his estate by its executor, his daughter (together ‘the complainant’).

The complainant said:

The financial firm said it met its obligations under the SEQUAL Code, and the loan was appropriately managed and paid out.

  • the financial firm failed to ensure the complainant obtained independent legal advice prior to entering the loan, in breach of its obligations under the Senior Australian Equity Release Association of Lenders (SEQUAL) Code of Conduct (the SEQUAL Code)
  • had the complainant been provided with independent legal advice, he would not have entered the loan
  • as a result, the loan should not have been provided
  • the estate should be paid $706,000 being the full amount of the equity release loan which was paid out on 28 September 2023.

Outcome

The Ombudsman found the complainant’s claim rested on the non‑provision of a single document from 20 years ago, the Certificate of Independent Legal Advice.

The Ombudsman was satisfied there was sufficient contemporaneous documentation available from the broker and solicitor’s diary notes to establish that the complainant had likely obtained independent legal advice in 2005 and the financial firm had a reasonable basis to be satisfied of this. Even if this had not been the case, the remedy sought by the complainant would not have been available, as the complainant had the benefit of the released funds.

The determination was therefore in favour of the financial firm. This complaint highlights the practical approach AFCA takes to assessing available information and its application to each claim for compensation.

Case study

Lender and broker involved in unsuitable loan

Background

The complainant obtained a second‑hand car loan for $11,945 with a broker over the phone.

Complaint

English was not the complainant’s first language, and she did not understand the application. She said the lender and broker had not met their responsible lending obligations and this resulted in substantial hardship for her in meeting repayments.

Outcome

The Ombudsman reviewed all the available information which included looking at how the bank assessed the complainant’s suitability for the loan. Bank statements provided to the lender showed the complainant’s expenses exceeded her income, with declining monthly savings, particularly just before the loan application.

Despite this, the lender assessed the complainant as having an uncommitted monthly income surplus after factoring in an extra $514 in loan repayments.

The complainant’s fortnightly expenses were over $3,000 compared to $1,287 in income. The Ombudsman found the lender’s assessment was unreasonable and that the loan was unsuitable, which meant that the lender failed to meet their responsible lending obligations.

Because the complainant had repaid more than the original loan amount to buy the car, her losses exceeded her benefits.

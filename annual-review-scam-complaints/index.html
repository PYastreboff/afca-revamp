Key complaint trends

Scam‑related complaints to AFCA have fallen 45% from the high levels of 2023‑24. During 2024‑25, we averaged 498 complaints a month. While this decrease is very welcome, it is important to remember these levels are still higher than during 2022‑23, and continue to remain too high.

The main scams we saw this year included:

When considering a scam complaint, AFCA currently needs to consider whether the transactions in dispute are authorised (made by the complainant with their knowledge and consent) or unauthorised (made by a scammer). We are required to determine whether a complainant is entitled to compensation by considering how the transaction(s) were made and the circumstances surrounding them. For more complex complainants – or matters involving significant loss – a panel made up of an ombudsman, and a representative from both industry and consumers will assess and determine the matter.

  • product/service (or buying/selling) scams – mostly linked to social media platforms like Facebook Marketplace
  • impersonation scams – usually through phone or SMS where scammers pretend to be from entities in authority like banks, Government agencies, telecommunication providers or online platforms
  • business email compromise scams – usually involving large payments, the scammer intercepts a genuine email and changes the payment details, or redirects the victim to deal with them, by inserting a similar looking email with a similar address into a chain of emails.

Resolution and timeframes

The majority of scam complaints are resolved early in our process — during the Registration and Referral stage — without the need for formal investigation. We consider this to be positive but believe some of these matters could have been resolved with consumers before the complaint was lodged. Overall, only around 5% of complaints proceed to Determination stage.

AFCA considers that current law is inadequate and fails to provide adequate protection for consumers, or to provide for redress in many situations. AFCA is very aware of the intense emotional and financial distress experienced by victims.

Our experience across scam complaints underscores the importance of early prevention and detection by financial firms. Once a scam occurs, early intervention and collaborative efforts are required to reduce complaint volumes and deliver timely, and effective outcomes for consumers.

Our approach to scam complaints

During 2024‑25, AFCA developed and published three scam‑related fact sheets to support greater transparency and consistency in how scam complaints are assessed. These resources help both industry and consumers to understand AFCA’s approach to its decision‑making, setting clearer expectations around outcomes and reinforcing our commitment to accessible, equitable resolution processes.

We also continue to publish and highlight ‘lead decisions’ that illustrate an approach in relation to specific fact scenarios.

Industry’s response to evolving scam trends

Scammers are generally part of sophisticated global crime syndicates and rapidly evolve their tactics to take advantage of new technology and ways of deceiving people.

AFCA has a role in sharing its complaint experience with our stakeholders – including industry, regulators and consumer advocates – to highlight gaps in the current law and codes. We support and encourage the initiatives we see banks introducing to detect and prevent scams. The rollout of ‘Confirmation of Payee’ across all banks and payment channels is a promising step toward reducing certain scam types and reducing scams complaints although at the time of this report, this still had not been implemented by all banks. More can and must be done across the banks, telecommunications companies and social media firms to implement stronger safeguards.

AFCA actively supports the work of the National Anti‑Scam Centre, including via its working groups, concentrating on specific areas such as emerging trends, targeted education campaigns and enhanced data sharing. Greater consistency and collaboration across the sector will be key to supporting the Scam Prevention Framework.

Scam Prevention Framework and increased jurisdiction

The Government has expressed its intention that AFCA will be designated as the sole EDR scheme under the Scam Prevention Framework. Over 2024‑25, AFCA has undertaken preparatory work to be ready for implementation once the Government announces the scheme’s commencement (see page 32 for more).

Expanding our stakeholder network

In addition to contributing to the development of the Scams Prevention Framework through formal submissions to Government and participation in working groups with the National Anti‑Scam Centre, AFCA has intensified its stakeholder engagement on scams.

By convening regular discussions with member firms, consumer advocates, regulators and international ombudsman schemes, AFCA has been able to identify emerging scam trends, share insights and provide analysis of our complaint data. These efforts have directly contributed to prevention strategies, improved complaint resolution outcomes and a reduction in complaint volumes.

The case studies included below focus on our different approach to scam transactions where the transaction is agreed to be, or is found to be unauthorised and the ePayments Code applies, or where it is agreed to be, or found to be authorised.

Case study

Unauthorised transaction and consideration of ePayments Code

Background

The complainant was contacted by someone pretending to be a well‑known online payment platform. The scammer told the complainant her money was unsafe with Bank A and convinced her to open an account with Bank B. She transferred close to $300,000 into her new Bank B account.

The scammer registered for online banking with Bank B using the complainant’s credentials and downloaded Bank B’s security app. He then used the app to generate passcodes to authenticate transactions. Over six days he made six disputed transactions of about $50,000 each from the woman’s Bank B account into a third‑party account.

The complainant discovered her money had been stolen when she went to a Bank B branch the day the final transaction was made.

Complaint

The complainant wanted Bank B to reimburse her for the nearly $300,000 loss. The bank accepted that the disputed transactions were unauthorised and made by the scammer and that they did not have to reimburse her because she had disclosed her passcodes, allowing for the scammer to transfer her money.

Outcome

An AFCA panel convened. As the disputed transactions were unauthorised, they considered the liability provisions of the ePayments Code. The available information showed:

The complainant did not agree to the use of online banking or Bank B’s security app – the scammer did. The ePayments Code says a consumer is not liable for any loss incurred on a facility the customer and bank have not agreed to use for account access (clause 11.2(b)(iv) of the ePayments Code).

The panel applied the terms of the ePayments Code to a novel scam situation and found the complainant was not liable for the disputed transactions. They required the bank to reimburse her in full, together with an award for non‑financial loss compensation.

  • the disputed transactions were made using the online banking password, and a one‑time passcode generated by Bank B’s security app for each disputed transaction
  • it was likely on the basis of the information provided that the complainant had disclosed her online banking password to the scammer, breaching ePayments Code security requirements
  • the scammer registered for online banking and downloaded Bank B’s security app.

Case study

Authorised transaction

Background

The complainant was purchasing a property and needed to pay $730,000. As settlement approached, she received an email ‘from her solicitor’, directing her to send the money to a third‑party account. Before the transfer, the complainant contacted her bank manager to ask some questions. She then made three transfers over three days, totalling $730,000.

The day after the third transfer, the receiving bank (Bank X) contacted the complainant’s bank to ask if the transfers were genuine. It became clear, the ‘solicitor’s email’ was fake and the third‑party account did not belong to the solicitor. The complainant was a victim of a business email compromise scam.

Bank X was able to retrieve $630,010 and return it to the complainant.

Complaint

This complainant wanted her bank to reimburse the remaining $99,990 that was stolen from her. She said the transfers were unauthorised because she intended them to be paid into her solicitor’s account – not an unknown third‑party account. She said the bank had failed in its duty of care and should have protected her from being scammed.

Outcome

AFCA’s determination was in the bank’s favour. The ombudsman found the complainant had authorised the transfers. She logged on to her banking app using her biometrics, input the Bank X account details and answered ‘Yes’ to the ‘SMS’, confirming it was her making the transfers. This case illustrates when a transaction will be considered authorised. The fact she did not intend to send money to a scammer did not make the transactions unauthorised for the purposes of the ePayments Code.

A bank’s primary legal duty is to follow its customers’ authorised instructions. In some circumstances, a bank will be ‘put on inquiry’ about a potential scam and will be expected to refrain from processing transactions until it asks further questions.

The ombudsman considered whether the complainant’s bank had made an error. The complainant contacted the banker who had asked reasonable questions. There was nothing about the interaction that put the bank on notice of the scam. The bank knew nothing about the Bank X account that would make it stop the transfers. The ombudsman was very sympathetic to the complainant’s circumstances, but as the bank was not at fault under the law or any code, no compensation was awarded.

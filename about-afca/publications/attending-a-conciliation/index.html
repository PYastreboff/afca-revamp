What is conciliation and how can it help me?

We organise a call that includes the complainant, the financial firm, and an AFCA conciliator to talk about the complaint in an open and informal way. We refer to this call as a conciliation throughout this fact sheet.

A conciliation is simply a guided conversation between yourself (the complainant), the financial firm and AFCA, where the shared goal is to resolve the complaint. Conciliation is one of the ways AFCA can resolve complaints, and in our experience, it can be the fastest and most effective way.

Most of the conciliations that we do are conducted by Microsoft Teams, with audio only. Although we also do video conciliations when appropriate and maybe face to face in exceptional circumstances. You do not need to download Microsoft Teams to participate or have any experience with technology. If you prefer, we can call you into the Microsoft Teams conciliation like a regular phone call.

Our conciliators are independent and skilled in mediation. Having an experienced person guide the conversation makes it easier for everyone to talk about the complaint.

The aim of a conciliation is to try to resolve the complaint by agreement on the day. This doesn’t always happen, but we find a conciliation can be an effective way for everyone to gain a better understanding of the issues and circumstances.

Who decides if a conciliation will be held?

When a complaint is assigned to an AFCA caseworker, they will review the facts of the complaint and decide whether a conciliation could help.

If the complaint is about superannuation, we may in some cases require the complainant to attend a conciliation. We will do this only if we think it is important that they attend, and we will always talk to them about it first.

Alternatively, complainants can ask us to organise a conciliation. Financial firms can also request a conciliation. We will consider these requests and talk to the complainant or the financial firm about whether this is a useful option.

Most conciliations take between one to two hours, but they may take longer. Participants can ask for a break at any time.

AFCA conciliation process

Can you tell me more about the conciliation process?

Before the conciliation

  • We will contact the complainant and the financial firm to discuss the complaint, confirm the conciliation process and arrange a date and time for the conciliation.
  • If we can’t reach the complainant, we will choose a date and time and confirm it in writing. If they cannot attend on that date and time, they need let us know as soon as possible.
  • We will support all parties by taking the time to explain the process and make sure everyone knows what to expect during the conciliation.

Ways you can join the conciliation

The conciliation will take place using Microsoft Teams.

You can join the conciliation a few different ways, depending on your preference:

Please let your AFCA caseworker know which option you would like to use before the conciliation.

  • online via the MS Teams app (desktop or mobile) if you already have it, or web browser by clicking the link in your invitation,
  • by phone (dial in) by calling the phone number in the invitation. You will be asked to enter the conference ID and passcode, or
  • we can call you and add you to the conciliation. If you prefer this option, please let us know the best number to call at least a before.

During the conciliation

  • Early in the conciliation we will ask the complainant to summarise their complaint and the financial firm to outline its response.
  • The conciliation is an opportunity for complainants and financial firms to speak directly to each other, with the conciliator guiding the conversation.
  • In the conciliation we will agree to some ground rules about how everyone will conduct themselves, such as being respectful and listening to the other person.

The conciliator’s role

The conciliator will sometimes, but not always, be the same person who is managing the complaint at AFCA. During the conciliation, they will:

  • Welcome and introduce everyone.
  • Explain their role how the conciliation will proceed.
  • Identify the main concerns in the complaint and set an agenda.
  • Invite the complainant and the financial firm to talk about their concerns in more detail. The conciliator will help everyone to identify possible options to resolve the complaint, but they will not advocate for either the complainant or the financial firm.
  • Speak separately with the complainant and the financial firm to discuss and clarify questions or concerns raised.
  • Provide information about alternatives to resolving the complaint if needed (but they cannot provide legal advice).
  • Make sure everyone understands what they are agreeing to.
  • End the conciliation if the process is being misused by anyone.

What is expected of complainants and financial firms?

  • Participate in the conciliation at the arranged time.
  • Turn up with an open mind and genuine goal of trying to resolve the complaint.
  • Be prepared to speak openly about the complaint and any concerns.
  • Have the authority to resolve the complaint on the day. This might mean getting consent from another person to go ahead with a particular outcome.
  • Let us know in advance if exceptional circumstances mean that the conciliation cannot go ahead at the organised time, such as a medical emergency.

What happens when an agreement is reached at conciliation?

  • We will record the agreement and provide confirmation in writing afterwards.
  • If complainants have any concerns about the agreement, they must contact us within 7 days.
  • Sometimes the financial firm will prepare a settlement document for the complainant to sign. This should reflect what was agreed at the conciliation. If it doesn’t, complainants should let us know.
  • Remember, complainants do not have to agree to resolve the complaint in a conciliation.

What happens if an agreement is not reached at conciliation?

  • If an agreement is not reached in a conciliation or after further negotiations, it is likely that we will investigate the complaint further.
  • In some cases, we may be able to give the complainant and the financial firms an idea at the conciliation about what the outcome of our investigation is likely to be. In other cases, we might need to ask for more information before we can make a decision about the complaint.

What happens to the information disclosed during a conciliation?

Conciliations cannot be recorded. However, if the complainant and the financial firm resolve the complaint, we will make a recording of what was agreed.

If the complaint cannot be resolved at the conciliation, we may use some of the information disclosed if we go on to consider the complaint further. This can include any new information discussed, and areas of agreement.

Types of outcomes from conciliation

The complainant and the financial firm work out the outcome they want from the conciliation together

Some examples of possible outcomes are:

  • An agreement that resolves the complaint entirely or in part.
  • An agreement to provide further information.
  • An agreement about how the financial service will be provided to the complainant in the future.
  • An agreement to end the financial service provided to the complainant.
  • An apology to the complainant.
  • In financial difficulty cases, a repayment arrangement, or an agreed timeframe for the sale of assets which secure the loan, if the loan can’t be repaid any other way.

Do complainants need paid representation?

  • Complainants do not need legal representation or any other type of paid support when taking part in an AFCA conciliation.
  • They can choose to have someone, such as a lawyer, to represent them, but any costs incurred are the complainant’s responsibility.
  • If the complainant chooses to have a representative, they should still attend the conciliation themselves.

Can complainants have another person present?

Complainants may decide to have someone, such as a family member, partner or friend with them during the conciliation. If they choose to be supported by someone, they should tell us their name (and relevant contact information) beforehand.

Pre-conciliation checklist

Check that you are available on the date of the conciliation and allow enough time in case the call goes longer than two hours. Think about the key issues in the complaint, what you would like to say and what the potential solution could be. Think about whether you would like another person to sit in on the call for support. Make sure the person is available and let AFCA know. Consider the complaint from all angles, including the opposite point of view. Prepare a copy of all of the documents and correspondence that might be needed during the conciliation. Make sure you have the authority to agree to resolve the complaint on the day. This may mean getting consent from someone else to go ahead with a particular outcome.

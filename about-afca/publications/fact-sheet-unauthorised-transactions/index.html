The law and AFCA’s ability to deal with scam complaints

The law and AFCA’s ability to deal with scam complaints is changing.

On 12 March 2026, AFCA’s jurisdiction expanded, allowing us to consider scam-related complaints involving receiving banks and unauthorised accounts opened in a consumer’s name, including wLearn more the consumer making the complaint is not a customer of that bank.

In February 2025, the Australian Parliament legislated a new Scams Prevention Framework. This framework is yet to commence. Further information about the Scams Prevention Framework can be found Learn more.

Until changes from the Scams Prevention Framework take effect, the current law and codes (detailed below) will apply.

What is an unauthorised transaction?

A transaction is unauthorised if it is made by a third-party without the customer’s knowledge and consent.

Increasingly, AFCA is seeing complaints about unauthorised transactions which are the result of a scam (unauthorised transaction scams).

Unauthorised transaction scams usually occur when a third-party scammer manipulates or tricks a person into disclosing their personal information, passwords, and/or financial firm issued SMS codes (or other similar one-time passcodes). They may also involve the consumer providing the scammer with remote access to their computer or mobile phone.

If a customer disputes authorising the transaction(s), then AFCA will firstly decide if the transaction(s) were authorised or unauthorised, in accordance with our approach detailed below.

A transaction will not be unauthorised just because the customer thought they were paying a party other than the party they in fact paid, if they carried out the transaction themselves or authorised someone else to do so.

What are some common unauthorised transaction scams?

Examples of common unauthorised transaction scams are listed below.

How does AFCA assess liability for unauthorised transactions?

AFCA will first determine if the disputed transaction is more likely unauthorised

If a customer disputes a transaction because they say it is unauthorised, the first question for AFCA to determine is whether the transaction was unauthorised.

AFCA will ask both parties to provide information about the disputed transactions, which we will review and consider, to decide what is the most likely explanation for how the disputed transactions occurred. We will then form a view, on the balance of probability, whether the disputed transactions were more likely authorised or unauthorised. AFCA will place more weight on documents created at the time the disputed transactions were made when forming a view.

  • Authorised Transactions Factsheet

AFCA will consider if the ePayments Code applies to the transaction(s)

If the available information shows a disputed transaction is more likely unauthorised, AFCA will then determine whether the complainant or the financial firm is liable for the loss.

AFCA’s approach to determining liability for unauthorised transactions depends on whether the ePayments Code (Code) applies to the transaction(s). The Code is a voluntary code of conduct, and it is binding on subscribing members. It sets out the Rules for determining liability for unauthorised transactions which are:

For business facilities, or wLearn more the financial firm is not a subscriber to the Code, liability for an unauthorised transaction will be determined under the terms and conditions applicable to the account (which may or may not adopt the Code), the law, and good industry practice. AFCA will publish further guidance on how we assess liability for these matters.

  • initiated using electronic equipment (such as online banking or card transactions), and
  • performed using a facility which is not established primarily for business purposes.

Under the Code, AFCA will consider if the customer contributed to their loss

Whether or not a customer is liable for the loss arising from unauthorised transactions depends on whether the customer contributed to the loss by breaching the “passcode security requirements” set out in clause 12 of the Code.

The most common reasons a customer may be found liable for their loss under the Code is wLearn more they breach the passcode security requirements by:

  • ‘voluntarily’ disclosing passcodes needed to perform a transaction to a third party
  • keeping a written record of passcode(s) needed to perform a transaction on, or carried with a device, without making a reasonable attempt to disguise or protect the security of the passcode(s)
  • acting with “extreme carelessness” in failing to protect the security of all passcodes
  • delaying reporting the misuse, loss or theft of their device or the breach of the security of any passcodes.

The financial firm must prove on balance the customer contributed to the loss

The onus is on the financial firm to prove, on the balance of probability, that the customer contributed to their loss by breaching the passcode security requirements of the Code.

If multiple passcodes were required to perform the unauthorised transaction(s), then the financial firm must prove the customer’s breach of the passcode security requirements was more than 50% responsible for the losses, when assessed together with all the contributing causes.

In deciding whether a financial firm has proven on balance that the customer contributed to their loss, AFCA will consider the available information and all reasonable explanations for the unauthorised transaction(s) occurring.

AFCA will consider if any disclosure was made ‘voluntarily’

When determining whether the customer or the financial firm is liable for the unauthorised transaction under the Code, it is not enough for the financial firm to simply show the customer disclosed one or more passcode(s) needed to perform a transaction to a third-party.

Under clause 12 of the Code, AFCA must consider if the customer ‘voluntarily’ disclosed the passcode(s). AFCA recognises tLearn more are exceptional circumstances in which a customer’s disclosure of the relevant passcode(s) may not be done ‘voluntarily’, such that they are not liable for their loss under the Code.

AFCA may exercise a discretion under the Code to reduce a customer’s liability

AFCA may also reduce the customer’s liability if the unauthorised transaction exceeds the daily limit for the payment type used.

  • the financial firm did not apply a reasonable daily or other periodic transaction limit to the account, and
  • the security and reliability of the means used to verify the transaction was not sufficient to protect the customer from loss in the absence of a reasonable daily limit.

What types of remedies are available?

AFCA considers complaints based on what is fair in all the circumstances, having regard to legal principles, applicable industry codes or guidance, good industry practice, and previous decisions of AFCA or its predecessor schemes (which are not binding).

The cases referred to below are intended as examples only. It is important to note the outcome of an individual complaint depends on the specific facts and circumstances.

The type of remedy AFCA may award depends on the circumstances. WLearn more AFCA finds the financial firm has made an error or breached an obligation, AFCA may:

  • award compensation for a customer’s direct or indirect financial loss
  • award compensation for a customer’s non-financial loss
  • instruct the financial firm to issue an apology for any error or breach.

What information should each party provide?

AFCA adopts an inquisitorial approach and will ask each party to provide information that will allow it to investigate the complaint. The type of information AFCA will ask may vary, depending on the circumstances. The lists below are not intended to be exhaustive.

The customer will be asked to provide any details they know about the scam

Generally, AFCA may ask a customer the following:

  • To identify the transactions they dispute
  • To provide as much detail as possible about how the customer thinks the underlying scam may have occurred, such as whether the customer: had any interactions with a third party Did they purchase items for the first time from an unusual website? Did they recently download a new app or a QR Code? downloaded remote access software onto their mobile, computer or other devices received any suspicious SMSs or emails that contained links, and if so, what happened if they clicked on the link received any one-time passcodes (OTPs) from their bank while they were interacting with a third party. If so, did they disclose the OTP and why
  • If the customer had any telephone calls with third parties, to provide a statement of recollection detailing their conversation, including what questions were asked, what response was provided and any instructions the scammer gave
  • Whether the customer keeps a record of any passwords needed to log onto internet or mobile banking, and if so, where and how this record is kept
  • Whether the customer made a police report about the scam. If so, to provide a copy.
  • Did the customer keep a screen shot of any of the matters outlined above

The financial firm will be asked to provide all relevant documents it holds

AFCA will generally ask a financial firm the following:

  • Provide details about the disputed transaction(s), including: how each transaction was made (i.e. mobile banking, internet banking, or an in-branch transaction), and a copy of the actual payment instruction (if applicable) how many passcodes (if any) were needed to perform each disputed transaction
  • The transaction logs and internet banking logs for a few months before and after the disputed transactions, which will collectively show exactly how and when the disputed transaction(s) were made, including all account activity
  • If a credit card is involved, the card transaction logs for a few months before and after the disputed transactions
  • Where the transaction occurs in branch, the branch logs and any branch notes for a few months before and after the disputed transactions
  • Account statement(s) for a few months before and after the disputed transactions
  • Whether the financial firm accepts the transactions were unauthorised. If the financial firm says yes, we will ask it to explain why the customer should be liable for the unauthorised transactions (referencing the specific provisions of the ePayments Code or terms and conditions)
  • Copies of all correspondence, including emails, internet banking, mobile banking or SMS messages with the customer
  • Copies of call recordings between the financial firm and the customer which are relevant to the disputed transaction(s)
  • All contact notes, diary notes and scam/fraud investigation notes
  • The relevant terms and conditions applicable to the account and electronic banking, as at the time of the disputed transaction(s).

AFCA factsheet – Superannuation interdependency relationships

Top

This factsheet explains how AFCA considers whether a deceased superannuation fund member was in an interdependency relationship at the time of their death.

  • Approach to superannuation death benefit complaints(opens in new tab)

Why is it important to consider whether the deceased member was in an interdependency relationship?

When a superannuation fund member dies, the trustee must distribute the benefit payable on that member’s death (death benefit). Trustees must ensure that the death benefit is only paid to recipients who were the deceased member’s dependants, or their legal personal representative (LPR), unless they have been unable to locate a dependant or LPR after reasonable inquiry.

The meaning of ‘dependant’ is subject to the governing rules (generally the trust deed) of the relevant fund, and the definitions in the Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act 1993 (Cth) (SIS).

SIS restricts the people who can receive all or part of a superannuation death benefit to:

Under SIS, the term ‘dependant’ includes ‘any person with whom the person has an interdependency relationship’.

  • the member’s dependants; and
  • the member’s LPR.

Interdependency relationship

A person may be a dependant of a deceased member if they were in an interdependency relationship with the deceased at the date of death.

If an interdependency relationship existed, that person will be the deceased’s dependant, and therefore eligible to be considered in the distribution of the death benefit.

AFCA will look at each claim of an interdependency relationship based on the evidence about the circumstances.

Does the trust deed need to refer to an interdependency relationship?

Yes, the trust deed must contain a definition of ‘dependant’ that extends to a person who was in an interdependency relationship with a deceased member when they died.

This could be an express reference to an interdependency relationship, or the trust deed might incorporate the SIS definition of ‘dependant’. As explained above, the SIS definition says a dependant of a person includes any person with whom the person has an interdependency relationship.

What is an interdependency relationship?

All four criteria must be met for an interdependency relationship to have existed, unless the following exception applies.

If two people have a close personal relationship and the reason they do not meet all of the other criteria is because either or both of them suffer from a physical, intellectual or psychiatric disability, or because they are temporarily living apart (for example, because one of them is working overseas or is in gaol) they are still considered to be in an interdependency relationship.

If two people were not living together when one of them died, and the exception explained above does not apply, they cannot be found to have been in an interdependency relationship at that time.

  • they had a close personal relationship, and
  • they lived together, and
  • one or each of them provided the other with financial support, and
  • one or each of them provided the other with domestic support and personal care.

Factors to be considered in determining whether an interdependency relationship exists

The regulations made under SIS set out factors to be taken into account when considering whether two persons had an interdependency relationship.

The factors are:

The factors are very similar to the factors for a de facto spousal relationship with the addition of whether the relationship was intended to be permanent and whether the relationship was one of mere convenience. They suggest that a mere friend or flatmate would not be in a ‘close personal relationship’ of the kind required by the interdependency relationship definition. An interdependency relationship need not satisfy all of these factors, and each is to be given an appropriate weighting in the particular circumstances.

Two people do not have an interdependency relationship if one of them provides domestic support and personal care to the other under an employment contract or on behalf of another person or organisation such as a government agency or other organisation. However, the mere fact that a person is being paid a carer’s allowance or similar payment would not exclude them from being considered in an interdependency relationship.

  • the duration of the relationship
  • whether or not a sexual relationship existed
  • the ownership, use and acquisition of property
  • the degree of mutual commitment to a shared life
  • the care and support of children
  • the reputation and public aspects of the relationship
  • the degree of emotional support
  • the extent to which the relationship was one of mere convenience, and
  • any evidence suggesting that the parties intended the relationship to be permanent.

Is the normal parent and a child relationship usually considered an interdependency relationship?

It is not expected that children will generally be in an interdependency relationship with their parents.

It is reasonable to assume that, but for the death of the parent who was the fund member, the relationship between the parent and child would change significantly over time. If a child was living with their parents, it would be reasonable to expect the child would move out of the parents’ home at some stage. The degree of domestic support and personal care would also change over time. These factors are relevant to whether the parties intended these aspects of the relationship to be permanent.

However, the law does not exclude parent-child relationships from being interdependent. All of the circumstances of the relationship between the parent and child are relevant to determine if they satisfy the requirements for an interdependency relationship.

Circumstances may mean the relationship between the parent and child was one beyond that of a normal parent-child such that it satisfies the requirements for an interdependency relationship. This includes any disability or illness of one person that means their relationship with the other, including the degree of support provided, is one of interdependency.

For example, there may be an interdependency relationship where a child is disabled or chronically ill and is expected to continue living with the parent permanently, or a parent is elderly and frail and is expected to continue living with their child on a permanent basis. In these situations, a parent living with their child will usually provide extraordinary support which surpasses the support provided in a normal parent/child relationship. Equally, a child living with a parent requiring additional care or support may have a permanent commitment to caring for the parent for the parent’s lifetime. In these situations, there may be a sense of permanence and a mutual commitment to a shared life.

Case studies

Examples of interdependency relationships

Mother and terminally ill son

Joseph died at age 25. He lived with his mother Gwen at her home from the time he was diagnosed with a terminal illness at age 22.

Statutory declarations from family and friends and other evidence showed Joseph was unable to live independently, due to his illness. Gwen provided him with personal care and emotional, financial and domestic support from the time he moved in with her, with the level of support and care increasing significantly in the last months of his life.

Having regard to all the circumstances, AFCA would accept that the relationship, care and support between Joseph and Gwen went beyond a normal parent-child relationship, and they were in an interdependency relationship immediately before Joseph’s death. All four of the mandatory criteria were met, and there was a mutual commitment to a shared life and a permanence to the living and care arrangement between Joseph and Gwen. This meant Gwen could be considered as a potential recipient of Joseph’s death benefit.

Mother and disabled daughter

Emily became severely disabled in an accident and could not live independently. Her husband Paul could not care for her, and she was placed in a group home. However, the care provided there was not suitable for her emotional and social needs.

With Paul’s agreement, Emily’s mother Denise was granted guardianship over her daughter, and it was decided that Emily would live with Denise. Denise had her home modified so Emily could live permanently with her. Denise cared for Emily, prepared her meals, did her laundry, took her to all her medical appointments and managed her finances. Denise also paid for Emily to have physiotherapy and occupational therapy and covered all expenses not able to be met from Emily’s Centrelink pension.

Statutory declarations from family and friends and other evidence showed Denise and Emily both understood Emily’s life expectancy was limited, and they intended this arrangement to continue for the rest of Emily’s life.

Emily died and was survived by Paul and Denise.

Having regard to all the circumstances, AFCA would accept that the relationship, care and support between Denise and Emily went well beyond a normal parent-child relationship and they were in an interdependency relationship at the time of Emily’s death. All four of the mandatory criteria were met, and there was a mutual commitment to a shared life and a permanence to the living and care arrangement between Denise and Emily. This meant Denise could be considered as a potential recipient of Emily’s death benefit.

Nephew and aunt

Sam was Sharon’s nephew and lived with her for 16 years, until Sharon died after a short illness. The evidence indicated that Sharon and Sam had a close personal relationship despite some ups and downs. Sharon had spoken of moving but said there would always be a room for Sam.

Statutory declarations from Sam and from family and friends, and other evidence, showed Sam and Sharon enjoyed living together and relied on each other. Sam relied on Sharon to shop for him, do his laundry and make sure he kept to his health and fitness regime, and Sharon relied on Sam to do the cooking, any house repairs and some heavy cleaning, and keep the garden tidy. There was no evidence to indicate the living arrangement between them was not intended to be permanent. Sharon didn’t charge Sam any rent and covered most of the utility bills. As well as helping with cooking, maintenance, cleaning and gardening, Sam looked after Sharon when she was unwell, drove her to medical appointments and made sure she took her medication.

Having regard to all the circumstances, AFCA would accept that Sam and Sharon were in an interdependency relationship at the time of Sharon’s death. All four of the mandatory criteria were met, and there was a mutual commitment to a shared life and a permanence to the living and care arrangement between Sam and Sharon. This meant Sam could be considered as a potential recipient of Sharon’s death benefit.

Previous partners

Ryan and Tim had been in a long-standing spousal relationship which ended a few years before Ryan’s death. They maintained a close friendship, however, and started living together again when Ryan was diagnosed with a serious illness. Tim took care of Ryan throughout his illness, providing extensive financial and domestic support and personal care. Ryan died several months after his diagnosis.

Although both were aware Ryan’s life expectancy was limited because of his illness, the evidence showed the arrangement was intended to continue for Ryan’s lifetime. Given the long-term relationship between the two, and statutory declaration evidence from family and friends about Tim’s commitment to care for Ryan for the rest of Ryan’s life, the arrangement was not merely one of convenience.

Having regard to all of the circumstances, AFCA would accept that the relationship went beyond that of a short-term care arrangement and was an interdependency relationship at the time of Ryan’s death. Although the spousal relationship had ended between Ryan and Tim, all four of the mandatory criteria for an interdependency relationship were met. There was a mutual commitment to a shared life and a permanence to the living and care arrangement between Ryan and Tim. This meant Tim could be considered as a potential recipient of Ryan’s death benefit.

Example of a relationship not considered to be an interdependency relationship

Mother and adult son

James died at age 39. At the date of his death, he had been living for 8 months in a spousal relationship with his partner and her children. In the past, James had often lived with his mother, Susan, when he was in between relationships or was struggling financially. Susan claimed that she was in an interdependency relationship with James, because James had a room in Susan’s house that was always available for his use. James also had mail sent to that address and Susan often helped him out with his bills when he was unemployed.

The evidence showed that James and Susan had a close personal relationship and Susan was a loving and caring parent who provided James with financial and emotional support when he needed it.

However, the criteria for an interdependency relationship were not met. This is because Susan and James were not living together when James died, and no exception applied. James was in a relationship with his spouse and was living with her. While James may have always been welcome to stay in Susan’s home and she kept a room for him there, this was not where he was living when he died. This position did not change because James had some of his mail sent to Susan’s address. AFCA would consider the relationship between Susan and James to be a normal parent-child relationship, and not an interdependency relationship.

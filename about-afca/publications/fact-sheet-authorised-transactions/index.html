The law and AFCA’s ability to deal with scam complaints

The law and AFCA’s ability to deal with scam complaints is changing.

In February 2025, the Australian Parliament legislated a new Scams Prevention Framework. This framework is yet to commence. Further information about the Scams Prevention Framework can be found Learn more.

What is an authorised transaction?

A transaction is authorised if it is made by the customer or a third-party with the customer’s knowledge and consent.

AFCA often sees complaints about authorised transactions which were made by the customer in the course of a scam (authorised transaction scams).

Authorised transaction scams occur when a third-party scammer manipulates or tricks a person into making a payment to an account controlled by the scammer, either directly or through intermediary accounts or platforms.

If a customer disputes authorising the transaction(s), then AFCA will firstly need to decide if the transaction(s) were authorised or unauthorised. For information on how we determine whether a transaction is authorised or unauthorised, see our fact sheet on unauthorised transactions Learn more.

What are some common authorised transaction scams?

Examples of common authorised transaction scams are:

  • Romance scams
  • Investment scams
  • Impersonation scams
  • Invoice hacking or email compromise scams
  • Job or employment scams

How does AFCA assess liability for authorised transaction complaints?

A financial firm’s primary duty is to act on its customer’s instructions

WLearn more a customer has instructed their financial firm to make a payment, the financial firm’s primary duty (main obligation) is to act on its customer’s instructions by making the payment as requested as soon as possible.

However, tLearn more are some situations in which the financial firm may be required to ask the customer questions about the payment before processing it.

When considering a complaint about authorised transactions, AFCA will consider whether the financial firm should have made inquiries (asked questions) about the payment before processing it.

AFCA will consider whether the financial firm had a duty to inquire

The financial firm has a “duty to inquire” in circumstances wLearn more an honest and reasonable banker, with knowledge of the relevant facts, would consider tLearn more was a serious possibility the customer is being scammed.

The duty to inquire may arise depending on the circumstances

The duty to inquire depends on the circumstances surrounding the transaction, including whether it is made online, over the phone or in branch.

WLearn more a customer instructs their financial firm to process a transaction in person or over the phone, tLearn more is a greater opportunity to interact with the customer than when the transaction is online. AFCA will ask the parties to provide information about their interactions at the time, to assess whether tLearn more were any indicators present that should have put the financial firm on inquiry, such as the customer appearing confused about their instructions or appearing to be acting at the direction of a third-party.

WLearn more a customer’s instructions are made without the involvement of the financial firm’s staff (such as via online banking), tLearn more is less opportunity to inquire. However, the duty to inquire may arise in certain circumstances wLearn more the financial firm had special knowledge about the transaction or recipient account:

  • from a regulator or Scamwatch, or
  • due to its fraud detection system triggering an alert for the transaction(s).

What are financial firms doing to detect and prevent scams?

Financial firms have developed their own fraud detection systems

AFCA often sees complaints about a financial firm’s use of fraud detection systems, including concerns that a financial firm’s fraud systems are inadequate and should have detected a fraudulent transaction.

In response to the increasing occurrence of scams and fraudulent activity, financial firms have developed fraud detection systems to try to reduce the impact of fraud and scams on their customers. These systems are designed to assess the risk of a transaction based on certain parameters or ‘Rules’ set by the financial firm. In doing so, the financial firm must balance competing interests and choose between developing a system which has Rules that are too:

In the absence of any legislative framework for scam detection, AFCA cannot:

  • narrow (limiting the amount or type of transactions it identifies as potentially fraudulent)
  • broad (flagging genuine transactions as potentially fraudulent and interfering with its customers’ ability to access their money on demand).
  • review the adequacy of a financial firm’s scam detection systems, or
  • compel financial firms to have certain scam detection systems in place.

What types of remedies are available?

AFCA considers complaints based on what is fair in all the circumstances, having regard to legal principles, applicable industry codes or guidance, good industry practice, and previous decisions of AFCA or its predecessor schemes (which are not binding).

The law and AFCA’s ability to deal with scam complaints

The law and AFCA’s ability to deal with scam complaints is changing.

On 12 March 2026, AFCA’s jurisdiction expanded, allowing us to consider scam-related complaints involving receiving banks and unauthorised accounts opened in a consumer’s name, including wLearn more the consumer making the complaint is not a customer of that bank.

In February 2025, the Australian Parliament legislated a new Scams Prevention Framework. This framework is yet to commence. Further information about the Scams Prevention Framework can be found Learn more.

Until changes from the Scams Prevention Framework take effect, the current law and codes (detailed below) will apply.

  • Receiving Banks and Unauthorised Opening of Accounts.

AFCA’s jurisdiction

AFCA’s ability to consider a complaint about money lost in a scam is set out in our Rules, which can be found Learn more.

Under our Rules, AFCA currently cannot consider a complaint about a party that is not an AFCA member (such as a telecommunication provider or a digital platform provider).

  • Learn more

Complaints lodged before 12 March 2026

For complaints lodged with AFCA pre-12 March 2026, AFCA can consider a scam complaint when:

  • the financial firm is an AFCA member
  • there is a customer relationship between the complainant (consumer) and financial firm
  • it is lodged at AFCA within the required time, which is usually six years from when the consumer became aware they have suffered the loss, and
  • the claim does not exceed AFCA’s monetary limit.

Complaints lodged from 12 March 2026

For complaints lodged with AFCA from 12 March 2026, AFCA’s expanded jurisdiction will apply.

This means AFCA can consider scam-related complaints about a ‘bank’, even if they are not a customer of that bank, if the complaint is about the bank’s actions in:

The complaint must also meet AFCA’s eligibility criteria under our Rules.

  • receiving their funds as part of a scam (receiving bank), or
  • opening an account or credit facility in their name without their consent or authority (unauthorised account opening).

How AFCA investigates scam complaints

The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) defines a scam as “a way of tricking people into handing over money or personal details”. What are some common scam complaints?

Impersonation scams involve a scammer impersonating a trusted and well-known business, or a customer’s family or friends, to steal a customer’s money or personal information.

Often, the scammer poses as the customer’s bank, telecommunications provider, internet provider, Microsoft, Amazon, Australia Post, Linkt, or other well-known entities or people with authority such as the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) or the police.

Impersonation scams usually come in the form of spoofing scams (scam phone calls or SMSs from a ‘spoofed’ phone number) or phishing scams (scam links sent via SMS or email), which are discussed further below. Sometimes, an impersonation scam may originate as a cold-call from an unknown or ‘private’ number.

Another variety is the family impersonation or “Hi Mum” scam. Customers receive a text message that purports to be from a family member or friend who says they have lost their phone and need emergency funds.

Spoofing scams involve a scammer contacting a customer, for example by calling them or sending them an SMS message, in which they impersonate a representative of a reputable company. In spoofing cases, the scammer will ‘spoof’ (or copy) the genuine company’s phone number; making it appear as though the call/SMS is coming from the company phone number.

The scammer will then deceive the customer into:

Phishing scams involve a customer receiving a communication, for example an SMS or email, from a scammer who is posing as a reputable company, which contains a link to a fraudulent website.

If the customer clicks on the link, they are usually taken to a false website, application form or payment portal, which is designed to obtain personal or confidential information, including online banking login details, passwords and/or credit card details. The scammer may use this information to access the customer’s account and perform transactions without the customer’s knowledge or consent. Alternatively, the website may ask the customer to enter their banking details to pay for something such as a toll or shipping costs and gather information that can later be used to steal the customer’s funds.

Digital wallet scams involve a scammer registering the customer’s card details to a digital wallet on the scammer’s device such as a mobile phone device. This is possible because the scammer tricks the customer into disclosing one-time-password(s) which the bank sent the customer via SMS, or which are generated within the customer’s mobile banking application. Digital wallet scams are often combined with impersonation or phishing scams.

Many scams involve a scammer tricking a customer into granting remote access to their device, either computer or mobile phone. The scammer then uses this access method to obtain information and ultimately access the customer’s accounts.

Many remote access scams originate via an impersonation scam. They also commonly arise via a pop-up on the customer’s device which advises them their device is infected with a virus and directs them to contact a number for “technical support”. The number connects them to a scammer who convinces them to grant remote access to clean the device.

Investment scams involve a customer sending money to a scammer under the mistaken belief they are investing in a legitimate investment. Often, the scammer is an unknown third-party who the customer finds online (on fake investment websites or advertisements), or the scammer makes a cold-call. The scammer may provide the customer with convincing (but fake) prospectus documents, or coach the customer into opening a trading account with a genuine cryptocurrency platform. The scammer may have control of the cryptocurrency wallet, or they may direct the customer to transfer funds from the cryptocurrency wallet to the scammer.

In investment scams, it is common for the customer to see a return or their initial small investments which encourages them to invest more. The aim is to build trust to encourage the customer to send more money and get friends and family involved in the false investment opportunity.

In romance scams, a customer sends money to a scammer with whom they believe they are in a relationship, but they have not met in person.

These scams often take place over a long period of time, building up trust before asking for money under some pretext. Usually, the pretext may be that the scammer needs money urgently for travel or due to a medical emergency, or the scammer knows of an “investment opportunity” they want to share with the customer.

In invoice hacking or email compromise scams, a customer can receive an email containing a fake invoice from a scammer who is posing as a genuine business the customer has recently dealt with.

In some cases, the scammer has intercepted a genuine email from a business and has changed the account details in the invoice so the funds will be sent to an account the scammer directs. The customer thinks they are paying the invoice. But the funds are paid into an account controlled by scammers.

Job scams involve a scammer posing as an employer or recruitment agency and offering the customer a job opportunity that does not exist. The goal is to steal the customer’s money and personal information. Often, they ask the customer pay an upfront fee to ‘start’ the role, or to buy products and training material that are ‘necessary’ for the role.

Alternatively, the ‘job’ itself might involve the customer receiving third-party payments into their account, which they must transfer from their account to other accounts while keeping a small commission. The money received is generally from other victims of scams, and the customer’s account may be considered a “mule account”. A mule account is used by criminals to launder money obtained through illegal activities. Participating in money muling is a serious criminal offence in Australia.

Another form of job scam is a “task scam”, wLearn more a customer is asked to complete a series of ‘tasks’ (such as leaving online reviews) to earn money. The ‘tasks’ progress through different ‘levels’ designed to gain the customer’s trust, and the customer is required to deposit their own money to withdraw their ‘earnings’.

In a recovery scam, a recent victim of a scam, who has had their money stolen from them, will be contacted by a scammer offering to help them recover their money for an upfront fee. The scammer may be posing as a law firm or some other business which is purportedly uniquely experienced in recovering money lost to scams.

This recovery agent may seek an upfront fee to undertake the recovery of the victim’s funds. To add an air of legitimacy, they may also, or alternatively, agree to take a percentage of funds recovered.

In a product or service scam, the customer is tricked into providing funds to a scammer believing they are purchasing something. Common examples include cars, caravans and pets.

In an unexpected money scam, the customer is tricked into transferring money or giving personal information in order to receive a reward. Examples include a lottery, competition or unexpected inheritance.

  • transferring money to the scammer under a false pretext
  • disclosing passcode(s) to the scammer, and/or
  • giving the scammer remote access to their computer and/or mobile phone.

Using complaint insights to shape fairer financial services

A systemic issue is a matter that goes beyond a single complaint, with the potential to affect a broader group of consumers. At AFCA, individual complaints often highlight patterns or concerns that signal deeper issues in a financial firm’s practices, systems or culture.

Identifying and addressing systemic issues is a key part of AFCA’s role in the broader regulatory framework. While we are not a regulator, we play a vital role in protecting consumers – including those who have not lodged a complaint, but may still be affected. Early detection and resolution of systemic issues helps to reduce future complaints, strengthen industry practices and prevent harm.

In 2024‑25, we uncovered systemic failures in product design, compliance, consumer experience and financial firm responsiveness. We focused on non‑financial harms – including delays, poor communication and process breakdowns – that undermine consumer trust and often reflect deeper governance or accountability failures. By identifying early warning signs, we can act quickly and engage constructively with industry to help drive lasting improvements.

AFCA is required to report systemic issues under section 1052E of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) and ASIC Regulatory Guide 267 (RG 267). Where appropriate, we report these matters to ASIC, APRA, the OAIC, and the ATO, enabling timely regulatory oversight and action.

Influencing for prevention, not just resolution

AFCA’s systemic work is a powerful tool for change. By helping affected consumers, but also looking at the root causes of systemic issues and working with industry to fix them, we help improve practices, rebuild trust, and reduce the likelihood of future complaints. In this way, our work supports a fairer, more resilient and more responsive financial system.

Impact of our systemic issues work in 2024‑25

  • The systemic issues AFCA identified, investigated and resolved this year affected 527,391 consumers and small businesses across a range of financial products and services. We:
  • conducted 163 detailed systemic issue investigations
  • reported 105 systemic issues to regulators
  • secured over $7.3 million in refunds and financial remediation for affected customers
  • delivered meaningful non‑financial outcomes, including: correcting consumer credit files clearer, more transparent Product Disclosure Statements (PDSs) improved financial firm conduct in internal and EDR stronger product design and suitability practices better data handling and disclosure.
  • correcting consumer credit files
  • clearer, more transparent Product Disclosure Statements (PDSs)
  • improved financial firm conduct in internal and EDR
  • stronger product design and suitability practices
  • better data handling and disclosure.

Other regulatory reporting under section 1052E(1)–(3) of the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth)

AFCA also reported 456 matters to regulators that were not systemic issues:

  • three serious contraventions of the law
  • 448 instances of refusal or failure to do what an AFCA determination said: 47 involving active AFCA members 401 related to potential CSLR claims
  • one contravention of the governing rules of a superannuation or Approved Deposit Fund
  • four involving settlement conduct requiring further investigation.
  • 47 involving active AFCA members
  • 401 related to potential CSLR claims

Engaging with industry to drive better outcomes

Through proactive and constructive engagement with financial firms, peak bodies and industry associations we:

We also contributed to reform consultations and regulatory working groups to support continuous improvement across the sector.

Our collaborative efforts are helping to foster a shared commitment to fairer outcomes and encouraging firms to be more responsive.

  • highlighted emerging risks and trends from our complaints and systemic work
  • shared insights on recurring failure points and barriers to resolution
  • clarified expectations and supported financial firms in addressing root causes
  • collaborated on strengthening fraud prevention, improving claims handling, and enhancing accessibility for consumers in vulnerable circumstances.

An important role in an ever‑changing environment

Many reforms are underway, including the SPF, the expanding Consumer Data Right, rollout of the Financial Accountability Regime. There are also emerging reforms in cryptocurrency and payments systems. Our systemic issues work is a critical source of insight for Government, regulators and stakeholders – uncovering real‑time risks, informing regulatory developments and supporting policy reform.

Our 2025‑26 work will focus on:

AFCA’s systemic issues function continues to be a critical lever for fairness, transparency and accountability – ensuring complaints are resolved, but also learned from.

  • promoting Equity of Access and responsiveness for consumers experiencing vulnerability
  • continuing to prioritise non‑financial harm and systemic root causes
  • maintaining early, cooperative engagement with firms
  • using systemic insights to help shape policy discussions and regulatory action
  • strengthening partnerships with industry to drive continuous improvement.

Case study

Hardship compliance and decision‑making

Background

AFCA identified systemic weaknesses in one firm’s handling of hardship applications. Customers were receiving decline letters without proper consideration of updated information or access to escalation pathways. In some cases, system‑generated decline notices were triggered automatically by miscoded status entries, with no human oversight. These failures left customers experiencing vulnerability without the support required under section 72 of the National Credit Code.

Outcome

AFCA’s investigation found 228 instances of incorrect hardship decline notices issued between 2022 and 2024. Following AFCA’s engagement, the firm:

The changes strengthened the firm’s processes and provided fairer outcomes for customers. AFCA reported the matter to regulators and continues to monitor compliance.

  • contacted affected customers and reassessed declined requests where appropriate
  • refunded fees and charges arising from the errors
  • introduced additional system safeguards to prevent automation errors
  • enhanced staff training and escalation protocols for hardship requests.

Case study

Misleading Product Disclosure Statement structure in accidental damage cover

Background

AFCA investigated systemic issues in the PDS of an insurer’s accidental damage cover. The structure separated ‘We Cover’ and ‘We Don’t Cover’ sections from general exclusions, creating confusion about when exclusions applied. This lack of clarity meant many customers misunderstood their level of protection. It contributed to a 36% claim rejection rate and more than 150 complaints to AFCA.

Outcome

AFCA’s investigation confirmed that unclear documentation was driving significant consumer detriment. In response, the firm:

These reforms improved disclosure, provided redress to affected customers and helped to rebuild consumer trust. AFCA reported the matter to regulators and is monitoring the implementation of changes.

  • rolled out staff training to improve consistency of decision‑making.
